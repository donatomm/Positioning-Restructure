**April Dunford\'s Complete Positioning Process Framework**\-\-\-\--**Core Definition**

Positioning is providing the backdrop for the use of your product to your customer so they know:

- What the product is

- Why it matters

- Why they should buy it

As Dunford explains: \"Positioning means establishing the context for the use of your product, and selling your product in the right market helps provide that context.\"\-\-\-\--**Common Positioning Mistakes to Avoid**

1.  **Assuming Clarity**: Marketers think positioning is clear because it\'s clear to them

2.  **Rigid Original Vision**: Unable to see product evolution beyond original concept

3.  **Market Evolution Blindness**: Failing to adjust positioning when market changes

\-\-\-\--**The 12-Step Positioning ProcessPhase 1: Foundation SetupStep 1: Identify Your Best Customers**

- Focus on identifying what aspects make your top customers happiest

- Look for patterns in customer appreciation

- Key Question: \"What aspects of your top customers make them appreciate your product so much?\"

**Step 2: Form the Positioning Team**

**Team Composition:**

- Cross-departmental representation

- Led by main business strategizer (CEO/founder in startups, division leader in large companies)

- Include outside facilitator for objectivity

**Team Purpose:**

- Define current and new positioning

- Incorporate diverse departmental perspectives

- Achieve company-wide alignment

**Phase 2: Analysis & DiscoveryStep 3: Develop Open Mindset**

**Requirements:**

- Establish common understanding of positioning

- Release current positioning assumptions

- Encourage accommodation over assimilation

**Step 4: Map Competitive Alternatives**

**Process:**

- Identify what customers would use if your product didn\'t exist

- Focus on customer perspective, not internal view

- Rank alternatives from most to least common

- Consider both direct and indirect alternatives

**Step 5: Document Unique Attributes**

**Guidelines:**

- List concrete, verifiable features

- Avoid vague claims unless verified by independent reviewers

- Consider multiple perspectives:

  - Customer feedback

  - Technical capabilities

  - Service aspects

**Step 6: Map Value to Customer Needs**

**Two-Step Process:**

1.  Determine Feature Benefits:

    - What does each feature do practically?

    - How does it improve customer experience?

2.  Identify Value:

    - How does the benefit solve customer problems?

    - What is the ultimate outcome for the customer?

**Example Structure:**

Feature → Benefit → Value

(High-powered suction) → (Greater cleaning power) → (Consistently clean home)

**Step 6.5: Value Consolidation**

- Group similar values together

- Limit to 1-4 value groups

- Consider customer perspective in grouping

**Phase 3: Market StrategyStep 7: Target Customer Segmentation**

**Process Steps:**

1.  Identify groups most excited about your value proposition

2.  Define how to identify these groups (behaviors, characteristics)

3.  Make segments as specific as possible initially

**Step 8: Market Category Selection**

**Three Options:**

1.  **Existing Market**:

    - Well-understood by customers

    - Suitable for large companies

    - Requirements:

      - Market in favorable transition

      - No dominant leader

2.  **Market Subsegment**:

    - Better for smaller companies

    - Focus on specific:

      - Industry

      - Region

      - Customer group

    - Must prove superiority in subsegment

3.  **New Market**:

    - Highest difficulty

    - Highest potential reward

    - Requires customer education

    - Complete control of narrative

**Step 9: Trend Alignment (Optional)**

**Evaluation Criteria:**

- Does trend align with current offerings?

- Is trend relevant to your market?

- Does trend support core principles?

**Warning Signs:**

- Reduced message clarity

- Overshadowing of core product

- Unclear connection to trend

**Phase 4: Implementation & EvolutionStep 10: Internal Communication**

**Positioning Document Elements:**

- Product name and description

- Market/segment definition

- Competitive alternatives

- Unique features

- Customer value proposition

- Ideal customer characteristics

**Step 11: Market Execution**

**Two Components:**

1.  **Product Narrative:\**
    Problem → Current Solution Failures → Ideal Solution → Your Product → Value

2.  **Marketing Messaging:**

    - Create core message document

    - Adapt for different channels

    - Include clear calls to action

**Step 12: Monitoring and Adjustment**

**Review Every 6 Months for Changes In:**

- Competitor landscape

- Regulations

- Technology

- Customer preferences

- Economic conditions

\-\-\-\--**Success Metrics**

Monitor positioning effectiveness through:

1.  Market perception alignment

2.  Sales team adoption

3.  Customer understanding

4.  Competitive win rates

5.  Market share growth

\-\-\-\--**Key Principles to Remember**

1.  \"Position yourself or be positioned\"

2.  \"Value varies enormously between customer segments\"

3.  \"One segment\'s dream can be another segment\'s nightmare\"

4.  \"Strong positioning is the antibody to competitor positioning attempts\"

\-\-\-\--*Note: This framework is based on April Dunford\'s work in \"Obviously Awesome\" and related materials. Always adapt the process to your specific context and needs.*
