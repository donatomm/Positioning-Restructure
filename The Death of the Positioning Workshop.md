## **The Death of the Positioning Workshop**

This is a summary/takeaways section provided by Gemini 3 Flash (Gem) by DM

## **TL;DR:**

- Traditional positioning workshops often fail because they create \"alignment theatre\" rather than genuine, lasting strategic consensus.

- Power dynamics and loud voices frequently suppress the most valuable insights from quieter, yet highly qualified, participants.

- Effective alignment requires deep pre-work, one-on-one stakeholder interviews, and external market data rather than just internal group brainstorming.

## **Detailed Summary:**

### **The Failure of the Workshop Format**

- **Jon Itkin** argues that workshops are fundamentally flawed because the consensus they generate is \"fake\" and dissipates once the facilitator leaves.

- The format inherently favors extroverts and is distorted by the presence of high-ranking executives (HiPPOs), leading others to self-censor or perform for their superiors.

- High-cost workshops (\$90k range) often result in \"polished decks\" that sit unused because the team never truly bought into the conclusions.

### **Alternative Strategies for Success**

- Several participants suggest that the workshop should be the *end* of a process, not the beginning.

- Successful consultants focus on \"pre-workshop homework\" and one-on-one stakeholder interviews to uncover uncomfortable truths that won\'t be shared in a group setting.

- There is a strong emphasis on bringing in the \"buyer\'s reality\" to counter biased internal perspectives and \"alignment theatre.\"

## **Key Takeaways:**

### **Human Dynamics & Power**

- The core issue isn\'t the workshop itself, but the failure to account for power imbalances. True alignment requires creating a \"safe\" culture where dissent is encouraged before final decisions are made.

### **Methodological Shifts**

- Moving away from \"brainstorming\" toward \"final reviews.\" The work of positioning happens in the weeks of research, customer interviews, and quiet reflection preceding any group gathering.

Today, I kill one of my darlings. The positioning workshop.

I genuinely like running workshops. I like the energy and conversations they generate. I like thinking on the spot. I like teamwork. And I love whiteboards.

I've run more workshops than I can count, and I've been a participant, too. Many of them were genuinely great, productive experiences. So this is actually hard for me to reckon with.

They don't work. At least, not like they're supposed to.

Workshops are supposed to be facilitated group conversations that follow a carefully mapped structure, to help a group of people reach a basic consensus about how to solve a problem.

I have no problem with any of that.

But here's the reality: Workshop consensus is fake. It's a performance. And it dissipates as soon as the wheels pull up on the consultant's flight home.

Workshop conversations get twisted by two powerful forces: loud voices and power dynamics.

In workshop settings, extroverted, vocal people thrive. Whoever has the most chutzpah becomes a power center in the room. Regardless of the substance of what that person has to say.

Workshops are also a performance for the powerful. In a workshop room, there is a single person who can fire or promote everyone else present. And so people truncate and modulate their opinions based on how they think that person will respond.

The net net of this is that in workshops, many people with important things to say don't speak up. And a lot of people nod along in agreement....when they don't actually agree.

The sensation of alignment can be real....but it usually doesn't last. It's fake. Alignment theatre.

A lot of big, expensive workshops are run on the assumption that afterward, the whole team will simply run with the conclusions.

But what really happens....is not much. The consultant sends their (polished, very nicely done) deck. And it sits. Because the group was never really bought in. And if they disagreed, they didn't commit.

The doubters go into their Slack channels and quietly undermine whatever togetherness the workshop generated, and it's as if it never happened all. Minus the \$90,000 you spent, of course.

The solution is not to stop having group conversations. It's to think differently about how we run them, build alignment, and drive follow-through based on organizational decisions.

I'll be getting to that soon.

## Positioning: The Graveyard of Unmade Decisions

This is a summary/takeaways section provided by Gemini 3 Flash (Gem) by DM

## TL;DR:

- Vague websites are symptoms of \"decision debt\"---a chain of unresolved positioning choices regarding category, buyers, and competition.

- Companies often over-analyze their market category while making dangerous assumptions about who their \"best-fit\" buyer actually is.

- Strategy is not the ultimate deliverable; commitment to explicit, written decisions is the only way to move past \"expensive thinking.\"

## Detailed Summary:

### The Root Cause of Vague Messaging

- **Jon Itkin** posits that positioning is not one grand idea, but a sequence of specific decisions (Category, Buyer, Problem, Competition, Differentiator).

- Most companies fail because they \"never finish the thinking.\" They debate the \"fun\" parts (category) but outsource or assume the critical parts (competition and buyer profiles).

- The hardest part of positioning is not the creative work, but getting leadership to commit to a direction long enough to validate if it\'s correct.

### The GTM \"Autopsy\"

- **Emily Kramer** and others discuss how a website acts as a diagnostic tool for GTM (Go-To-Market) problems.

- Messy navigation, lack of pricing, and unclear value propositions are direct results of \"too many cooks\" in the kitchen and a lack of authority to say \"no\" to certain ideas.

- Not making a decision is, in itself, a decision with severe consequences for market power and conversion rates.

## Key Takeaways:

### Decision-Making as Strategy

- Positioning help is often actually \"decision-making help.\" Teams don\'t lack ideas; they lack the institutional willpower to pick one path and exclude others.

### The \"Commitment\" Metric

- Strategic success is tied to the duration of commitment. For B2B, this often means sticking to a decision through at least one full sales cycle or a specific number of closed deals to see real data.
