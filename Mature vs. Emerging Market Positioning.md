## **Mature Markets vs. Emerging Workflows** by A-PIERRI

This is a summary/takeaways section provided by Gemini 3 Flash (Gem) by DM

## **TL;DR:**

- **The Ultimate Tradeoff:** Companies must choose between positioning within a \"Mature Market\" (high demand, high competition) or an \"Immature Market\" (no category demand, but you define the rules).

- **The Conflict of \"New Categories\":** While founders love the idea of \"Blue Oceans,\" experts warn that creating a category is a \"double sale\"---you must sell the problem before you can sell your solution.

- **Positioning is Fluid:** Success requires treating positioning as a living decision; failing to adapt as a market matures leads to \"stalling at mid-stage\".

## **Detailed Summary:**

### **Mature vs. Immature Framework**

**Anthony Pierri** introduces a decision tree to help B2B companies navigate two distinct paths. **Mature Markets** (like CRM) offer existing budgets and high inbound demand, but require battling entrenched giants like Salesforce. **Immature Markets** center on workflows currently handled by manual processes; here, the competition is the \"status quo,\" and while there is no dedicated budget, the vendor has the power to set the price.

### **The Practical Realities of \"Category Creation\"**

The discussion highlights that most startups cannot afford to create a category from scratch. Participants emphasize that it is often more efficient to \"hijack\" an existing category with a radically better differentiator rather than trying to educate the market on a brand-new concept. The consensus leans toward finding an underserved sub-segment of an incumbent\'s market rather than going head-to-head or creating a \"greenfield\" spot.

## **Key Takeaways:**

### **Market Dynamics**

- **Demand Capture vs. Demand Generation:** In mature markets, you capture existing demand; in immature markets, you must generate it by aligning the problem with your solution.

- **The \"Double Sale\" Burden:** Positioning for a workflow requires selling the user on a new way of working *and* your specific tool, which is resource-intensive.

### **Strategic Execution**

- **The Budget Litmus Test:** If a buyer can't find a line item for your product without creating a new one, you are in an immature market.

- **Avoid the \"Everything\" Trap:** Trying to message to both mature and immature groups simultaneously results in \"selling to everyone and nobody\".

Or\... Should you position for an emerging, immature market? Mature markets center around defined product categories. Take CRM. • Companies shop for CRMs every day. • They have allocated budget. • They know the players. • They\'ve purchased software like this before. • They\'ll purchase software like this again. Incredibly mature and defined market\... that comes with tradeoffs. The only direct vendor level competition in these immature markets come from other small startups (and you probably want to pretend they don\'t exist, lest you accidentally give them free distribution). Your REAL competition is the status quo way of accomplishing the workflow (i.e. how everyone does it today). Again, there are tradeoffs: • No behemoths like Salesforce to compete with\... but also nobody is shopping for your type of tool (i.e. no inbound demand at the category level, because there is no category yet) • No dedicated budget BUT you get to set the price (i.e. you\'re not bound by the typical pricing of the category, because again\... no category exists)

The positioning, messaging, go-to-market strategies, etc. for these two markets (mature and immature) could not be more different.

Speaking to the mature group alienates the immature group, and vice versa.

Most companies need to pick where they want to play and where they think they can win.

Use this decision tree to help you decide if a mature, category-based approach is for you (and tell me where you land!!)
