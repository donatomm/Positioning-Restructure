# **Executive Alignment and Go-to-Market Transformation Through Structured Positioning Workshops**

## **Introduction to the Executive Positioning Intervention**

In the highly competitive and increasingly saturated landscape of Business-to-Business (B2B) Software as a Service (SaaS), product positioning is frequently, and erroneously, relegated to the domain of copywriting, brand messaging, or purely tactical marketing execution. However, an exhaustive analysis of first-hand accounts, direct testimonies, and implementation reports from executives who have undergone structured, facilitated positioning workshops—specifically those leveraging the methodology developed by industry expert April Dunford—reveals a drastically different and far more complex reality. In practice, a formal positioning workshop functions less as a marketing exercise and more as a foundational business strategy intervention that reverberates through every department of an organization.1

These structured engagements, which can command premium enterprise-level investments ranging from $80,000 to $100,000 for a multi-day facilitated session 3, force organizations to fundamentally reevaluate the core tenets of their business model. Participants are required to systematically deconstruct how they capture value, who their ideal and most profitable customers actually are, and how they navigate and define the competitive market categories in which they operate. The outputs of these workshops are not merely new taglines; they are entirely new go-to-market architectures, revitalized product roadmaps, and highly structured sales narratives designed to reduce buyer friction and accelerate revenue acquisition.

This comprehensive research report analyzes the practical application, internal dynamics, operational prerequisites, and long-term organizational outcomes of these positioning workshops. The analysis relies entirely on the testimonies, published case studies, and first-hand accounts of direct participants. By deconstructing the experiences of a diverse array of B2B technology companies—ranging from early-stage, agile startups like Userlist and Ventup, to established global entities and high-growth platforms like Skillsoft, Funnel, and Postman—this report provides a nuanced, empirical understanding of how positioning workshops operate in the real world.2 The subsequent sections will delve into the strict prerequisites for workshop success, the psychological and operational shifts within cross-functional teams, the mechanical realities of market category redefinition, and the long-term operational velocity gained when an entire executive team aligns around a singular, differentiated market narrative.

## **The Pathology of Poor Positioning Prior to the Workshop**

To understand the mechanics and the impact of the positioning workshop, it is necessary to examine the organizational pathology that drives companies to seek out this highly specialized intervention in the first place. Participant testimonies consistently highlight a state of internal gridlock, confused market perception, and sales inefficiency prior to undergoing the facilitated process.

### **The Misalignment of the Executive Suite**

A primary indicator that an organization requires a positioning intervention is the misalignment of the executive team regarding the product's core value. It is remarkably common for highly intelligent, deeply invested, and opinionated executive teams to fundamentally disagree on why their customers ultimately choose their product over alternatives.3 This internal dissonance does not remain contained within the boardroom; it manifests externally as confused marketing messaging, inconsistent sales pitches, and a disjointed user experience.

Fredrik Skantze, Chief Executive Officer and Co-Founder of Funnel, reflected extensively on this dynamic. He articulated that one of the most critical, yet frequently neglected, jobs of a CEO is to synthesize the company’s internal vision and technical capabilities into a singular, compelling point of view that the market can readily consume.3 Skantze described the workshop process as a "transformational" journey for his business, highlighting how the structured facilitation provided the necessary framework to unify his executive team's fragmented perspectives into a powerful, coherent narrative.3 Without this intervention, executive teams often operate in silos, with the product team building for one hypothetical user, the marketing team generating leads for another, and the sales team attempting to close deals with an entirely different buyer persona altogether.

### **The Trap of Feature-Focused Selling**

Another critical pathology observed in companies prior to the workshop is an overwhelming reliance on feature-focused selling. In the B2B SaaS environment, product and engineering teams frequently dominate the internal corporate narrative. Because these teams spend thousands of hours developing complex technical capabilities, there is a natural organizational bias toward showcasing these features in market-facing materials. Consequently, sales pitches begin to resemble exhaustive technical manuals or feature checklists rather than compelling business cases that address the buyer's underlying economic or operational pain points.8

Lindsey Padrino, Senior Vice President of Strategy & Business Operations at Skillsoft, provided direct testimony on the transformative nature of overcoming this trap. She noted that the workshop approach was the catalyst that finally helped the leadership, sales, and marketing teams align around differentiated business value, successfully and permanently moving the organization away from a rigid, ineffective "feature focus".2 The workshop achieves this by systematically demanding that every unique technical attribute be explicitly mapped to a specific, measurable value that a clearly defined customer segment cares deeply about.5 If a technical feature, no matter how complex or expensive to build, cannot be tied to a distinct business outcome that separates the company from its true competitive alternatives, the workshop framework strips it from the core positioning narrative.

## **Structural Prerequisites and Pre-Workshop Architecture**

The success of a multi-day positioning workshop is not derived solely from the activities that occur during the sessions; it is heavily dependent on the rigid structural conditions established before the participants even enter the room. First-hand accounts and practitioner descriptions indicate that the methodology strictly rejects the traditional consulting model, wherein an external advisor conducts independent, isolated market research and subsequently delivers a finalized, bound strategy document to the client.13 Instead, the workshop relies entirely on the extraction, structured processing, and synthesis of latent knowledge that is already possessed by the company's internal teams.

### **The Absolute Mandate for an Existing Sales Motion**

A critical and non-negotiable prerequisite for the premium positioning workshop, as experienced by participating organizations and articulated by facilitators, is the presence of an active sales team and a history of existing customer traction.11 These workshops are fundamentally designed to analyze the empirical reality of *why* current customers buy and why they stay, rather than hypothesizing in a vacuum about future, untested buyer behavior.

Direct accounts regarding the qualification process for these workshops note that engagements are often flatly refused or "hard disqualified" if a company lacks a dedicated sales function.11 The strategic reasoning underpinning this prerequisite is purely practical and deeply rooted in market reality. Without a sales team actively fielding objections, losing specific deals to identifiable competitors, and navigating the Byzantine procurement processes of enterprise buyers, the executive team simply lacks the raw, empirical data necessary to determine their true competitive alternatives and their actual differentiated value.11 As one account notes, companies that lack a sales team fundamentally do not know the answers to the questions that must be answered in order to successfully execute the positioning exercise.11 The workshop does not invent a market reality out of thin air; it codifies, structures, and refines the messy reality that the sales team is already experiencing on the front lines every day.

### **Aligning the Organizational Vocabulary Through Pre-Work**

To maximize the efficiency of the highly expensive in-room facilitation time, participating companies are often required to implement mandatory pre-workshop preparation. First-hand accounts from marketing professionals and workshop participants note the rigorous practice of assigning specific "homework" to the entire executive team. This typically involves reading foundational positioning materials, with specific chapters (such as chapters 1, 2, and 10 of relevant methodological texts) assigned to ensure baseline comprehension.14

This preparatory phase is not busywork; it is a critical alignment mechanism designed to establish a shared, precise vocabulary.5 In the corporate world, terms like "messaging," "branding," "category," and "positioning" are often conflated or used interchangeably.1 By forcing the executive team to adopt a strict definition of these terms prior to the engagement, the facilitator ensures that the workshop does not devolve into frustrating, time-consuming semantic debates, allowing the participants to focus entirely on strategic alignment and market realities.

### **The Cross-Functional Participation Mandate**

Furthermore, the assembly of the participants is strictly managed to ensure cross-functional representation. Case studies and participant reflections highlight that the workshop cannot, under any circumstances, be isolated to the marketing department alone. Effective execution and subsequent organizational buy-in require the active, continuous participation of the Chief Executive Officer, the head of Sales, Customer Success leadership, and Product Management.4 By forcing these historically distinct and often adversarial disciplines into a single room, the workshop structurally eliminates siloed perspectives. This cross-functional crucible ensures that the resulting positioning strategy is mathematically and operationally viable across all stages of the customer lifecycle—from the initial marketing acquisition and lead generation, through the complex sales closure process, and into long-term customer retention and product adoption.

## **The Facilitation Philosophy: Guiding Over Dictating**

The internal dynamics of the positioning workshop represent a significant departure from standard corporate strategy sessions or outsourced marketing agency engagements. Participant feedback consistently highlights the specific role of the facilitator as a structural guide and an objective mediator, rather than a subject matter expert on the specific software product being positioned.13

### **The Rejection of "Doer Work"**

The methodological philosophy underpinning these workshops explicitly rejects the concept of "doer work"—the idea that an external consultant will independently write the positioning statement or craft the messaging in isolation.13 As discussed in industry analyses regarding the evolution of consulting practices, the executive team inherently knows vastly more about their product's technical capabilities, their specific market nuances, and their historical sales challenges than any external consultant could ever learn, even given months of dedicated research.13

Therefore, the profound value of the workshop lies not in the facilitator providing the answers, but in the facilitator providing a rigorous, battle-tested framework to extract those answers from the team.13 The facilitator acts as a forcing function, utilizing the structured process to pull the latent knowledge out of the executive team, challenging their assumptions, breaking ties when executives disagree, and organizing the resulting insights into a highly coherent, actionable narrative.13

### **The Value of the External Moderator**

The necessity of this facilitation dynamic was deeply explored in discussions between industry experts Nathan Barry and Katelyn Bourgoin, who noted that organizations do not merely want to purchase a static course or a theoretical book on positioning; they fundamentally desire the active facilitation process.16 They require a trusted, objective third party who can manage the complex, often ego-driven interpersonal dynamics of an executive team.

In a workshop setting, a founder might be deeply attached to a specific feature they spent years coding, while the head of sales might view that same feature as irrelevant to closing deals. An internal employee, even a senior marketing executive, often lacks the political capital to tell the CEO that their pet feature is commercially useless. An external facilitator, wielding a proven methodology, can navigate this political minefield objectively, forcing the team to base their positioning decisions on empirical customer data rather than internal corporate hierarchy.16

## **The Tactical Mechanics of the Workshop Experience**

While the specific agendas may vary slightly depending on the scale of the organization, the core mechanics of the positioning workshop follow a highly structured, sequential pathway. This pathway is designed to break down legacy assumptions and rebuild the company's narrative from the ground up, based entirely on the perspective of the buyer.

### **Phase 1: Identifying the Customers Who Truly Love the Product**

The foundational step of the workshop exercise requires the cross-functional team to compile a shortlist of their absolute best customers. As noted in the implementation logs of companies like Userlist, the criteria for this list are strict: these must be customers who understood the product's value proposition immediately, navigated the sales process quickly, became raving fans, and eagerly provided referrals or acted as references.5

This exercise forces the organization to look past their total user base—which often includes legacy customers, highly demanding outlier clients, or users who generate low revenue—and isolate the specific characteristics of the buyers who represent the company's ideal future state.5 By hyper-focusing on the psychological, firmographic, and operational traits of these "best-fit" customers, the team establishes the baseline for all subsequent positioning decisions.

### **Phase 2: Isolating the True Competitive Alternatives**

Once the best customers are identified, the workshop forces the team to answer a surprisingly difficult question: *If our product did not exist tomorrow, what would these specific customers do?*.5

This phase frequently triggers profound realizations within the executive team. Companies often define their competitors as the other software startups in their specific niche. However, the workshop methodology demands a broader view of the status quo. For many B2B SaaS companies, the true competitive alternative is not a rival software platform; it is a disorganized collection of Excel spreadsheets, a manual process executed by an intern, or simply the customer deciding to do nothing at all out of fear of implementation failure.4 By accurately mapping the true alternatives, the team can finally understand the exact baseline against which their product is being judged by the market.

### **Phase 3: Translating Unique Attributes into Differentiated Value**

With the true alternatives established, the cross-functional team is tasked with listing every unique attribute or feature their product possesses that the alternatives do not.5 However, as previously discussed, features alone do not close deals. The crucial mechanical step of the workshop involves translating these raw technical attributes into "value themes".5

For every unique feature, the team must ask, *So what? Why does the buyer care about this?*.9 This translation exercise bridges the gap between the engineering department and the sales department, transforming technical specifications into measurable business outcomes, such as reduced operational risk, increased revenue velocity, or enhanced regulatory compliance.

### **Phase 4: Defining the Market Category**

The climax of the workshop often revolves around the selection of the market category. The category is the mental heuristic the buyer uses to understand what the product is, how much it should cost, and what features it should possess.4 If a product is positioned in the wrong category, the buyer brings a set of expectations that the product cannot meet, leading to immediate disqualification. The workshop process demands that the team select a category—or, in rare cases, create a new one—that places their specific differentiated value at the absolute center of the buyer's evaluation criteria.5

## **Deconstructing the Transformation: The Ventup Case Study**

To thoroughly understand the tactical mechanics, the emotional shifts, and the profound ripple effects of the positioning workshop, it is necessary to examine detailed, first-hand implementation accounts. Paco Martínez's exhaustive documentation of implementing the methodology at Ventup provides a masterclass in how the workshop functions in real-time and how it permanently alters a company's go-to-market trajectory.4

### **The Assembly and the Segment Narrowing**

At Ventup, the implementation began precisely according to the methodological mandate: assembling a multidisciplinary team. Martínez, representing Product and Marketing, brought together the heads of Sales and Customer Success, along with the co-founders.4 This structural decision was paramount; it ensured that the workshop's outputs would not be viewed as a mere "marketing project," but rather a core business strategy with immediate buy-in across all revenue-generating departments.

The team's first critical decision within the workshop framework was to drastically narrow their focus. Rather than attempting to position their software for the entire, sprawling food and beverage industry, they actively constrained their scope to a single customer segment: Small and Medium-Sized Business (SMB) Restaurants.4 This segment was chosen strategically because it represented the largest total addressable market, was highly reachable via traditional marketing channels, and was a segment the company was already targeting aggressively.4 By constraining the focus, the workshop exercises became hyper-specific, preventing the dilution of value that inevitably occurs when a company attempts to position a complex product for disparate use cases simultaneously.

### **The Benchmarking Epiphany and the Category Revelation**

The most profound realization during the Ventup workshop occurred during the benchmarking and category analysis phase. Through solo, heads-down research on competitor terminology and deep analysis of customer feedback, Martínez uncovered a fatal, structural flaw in how the market perceived their product.4 Ventup was consistently being evaluated by prospects as a general "restaurant management tool." This categorization forced them into direct, brutal competition with established, heavily funded Point of Sale (POS) and Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) systems.4

Because prospective buyers were using the mental heuristic of a "restaurant management tool," they inherently expected specific operational features, such as table booking capabilities and front-of-house management.4 When buyers discovered during the sales process that Ventup lacked these features, the product was immediately disqualified, leading to lost deals and immense friction in the sales pipeline.4 The sales team was constantly fighting an uphill, unwinnable battle against an incorrect set of customer expectations.

### **The Radical Category Shift**

The workshop methodology dictates that if a product is consistently losing deals because it is being judged against the wrong criteria, the market category must be changed to set the proper context. For Ventup, the cross-functional team proposed a radical, surgical shift: abandoning the "restaurant management" category entirely and repositioning the product strictly and unapologetically as "procurement software for restaurants".4

This strategic pivot, born directly from the workshop's structured exercises, instantly reframed the product's value proposition. By defining themselves as procurement software, the absence of table booking features was no longer a disqualifying factor; it was entirely irrelevant to the category.4 Instead, Ventup's specific, highly developed strengths in supplier management, automated ordering, and inventory purchasing—their true differentiated value—were thrust to the center of the buyer's evaluation criteria.4

| Evaluation Vector | Pre-Workshop: "Restaurant Management" | Post-Workshop: "Procurement Software" |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Primary Competitors** | Massive POS & ERP systems 4 | Manual processes, spreadsheets, niche supply chain tools 4 |
| **Buyer Expectation** | Table booking, front-of-house operations 4 | Supplier relations, inventory efficiency 4 |
| **Sales Friction** | High (constantly defending lack of POS features) 4 | Low (focusing entirely on procurement pain points) 4 |
| **Win Rate Driver** | Attempting parity with behemoths 4 | Demonstrating superiority in specialized procurement 4 |

### **Tangible Outputs and the AI-Driven Execution**

The outputs of the Ventup workshop were not left as abstract concepts or theoretical whiteboards. The team captured the new positioning theory in highly actionable, immutable formats. They developed a comprehensive positioning strategy one-pager and a master draft sales pitch hosted in Notion, which became the singular source of truth for all subsequent corporate communications and strategic decisions.4

This centralized alignment enabled rapid, coordinated execution across the organization. The marketing team initiated week-long design sprints to entirely overhaul the SMB Restaurant section of their public website, meticulously rewriting the copy to reflect the new category and highlight their "procurement superpowers".4 The sales deck was completely restructured using a storyboard approach derived directly from the workshop's sales pitch framework, ensuring that the narrative flowed logically from market insight to differentiated value without devolving into a feature dump.4

Furthermore, the strategic clarity generated by the workshop allowed the team to leverage emerging Artificial Intelligence tools with unprecedented efficiency. Because they knew exactly who they were talking to and what value they provided, their prompts became highly precise. They utilized Midjourney to generate specific, photo-realistic assets of target markets in working environments (such as commercial kitchens, bars, and warehouses) to build instant visual rapport.4 ChatGPT was deployed to synthesize workshop insights into shareable, high-impact social media snippets.4 Turboscribe and Veed were used to rapidly process and auto-caption interview-style video clips featuring the Chief Product Officer narrating stories about specific industry pain points.4

### **The Human and Organizational Impact**

The success of the positioning workshop is ultimately measured not just by new collateral, but by its impact on the human capital within the organization. At Ventup, the founders reported a profound sense of excitement and relief; the workshop had finally articulated their complex, overarching vision in "words anyone can understand".4 Crucially, as the company scaled, the founders were no longer the single point of failure for explaining the product's value to the market; the framework empowered the entire team.4

For the sales team, the psychological shift was palpable. Prior to the workshop, sales representatives struggled with the massive cognitive load of constantly defending the product against POS system comparisons.4 Following the implementation, the sales team reported feeling deeply "comfortable and proud" of the new narrative and the accompanying sales materials.4 Martínez noted a distinct "compound effect" on organizational speed: while the initial learning curve of the workshop methodology required slow, deliberate, intellectually taxing work, the resulting common language eventually allowed all teams to communicate core messages rapidly and consistently across every single channel.4

## **Confronting the Incumbent: The Userlist Experience**

The necessity of confronting uncomfortable market realities during the positioning workshop is further illustrated by the experience of Userlist, a SaaS platform specifically designed for B2B companies. Like many startups, Userlist found themselves struggling to communicate their exact value proposition in a highly crowded, deeply commoditized software ecosystem.5

### **Acknowledging the "Elephant in the Room"**

Prior to their positioning overhaul, Userlist described themselves to the market using the broad category of "email automation".5 While technically accurate regarding the mechanical function of the product, this positioning was strategically disastrous. It placed them in a massive ocean of marketing software, forcing them to compete against generalized, low-cost tools that did not share their specific, nuanced B2B focus.

Through the application of the structured positioning method—driven by co-founder Claire Suellentrop, who integrated the workshop concepts after attending an industry conference—the Userlist team was forced to execute the most uncomfortable step of the process: explicitly listing their true competitive alternatives.5 In doing so, they had to confront a massive market incumbent: Intercom.5

Initially, the team felt that explicitly naming a giant, heavily funded incumbent like Intercom in their marketing and sales narratives was too risky, presumptuous, or taboo.5 Intercom was the "elephant in the room" that everyone internally knew they were competing against in deals, but no one wanted to acknowledge it out loud in their public positioning.5

### **The Power of Explicit Differentiation**

The workshop principles fundamentally altered this timid mindset. The methodology teaches that by explicitly acknowledging the dominant competitive alternative, a company can rapidly set the context for the buyer, anchor their expectations, and immediately pivot to their specific differentiated value.5

Userlist embraced this aggressive strategy, executing a complete positioning change from the generic "email automation" to the highly specific "customer messaging".5 Crucially, they began calling out their primary competitors, especially Intercom, by name in their market positioning.5 They were supported in this decision by external validation, including advice referencing the success of tools like Tom's Planner, which successfully positioned itself by explicitly defining what it was *not* (not a complex MS Project tool, not a basic spreadsheet).5

The effect of this strategic clarity was immediate and profound. By defining themselves explicitly against a known entity, potential customers could instantly grasp exactly what Userlist did, while simultaneously understanding why Userlist might be a superior fit for their specific, B2B-focused needs compared to the generalized, heavy incumbent.5 This clarity not only resolved intense friction in the customer acquisition process but also translated into immense, tangible investor confidence; the refined, highly articulate positioning was instrumental as the company successfully raised funding from 21 angel investors over a tight four-month period.5

## **Transitioning Go-To-Market Motions: Postman and the Enterprise PLG Dilemma**

Positioning workshops are not solely the domain of early-stage startups searching for initial product-market fit; they are critical strategic levers for highly mature, hyper-growth companies attempting to transition or scale their Go-To-Market (GTM) motions. A prime example of this application is Postman, the globally adopted API platform. Abhinav Asthana, Co-Founder and CEO of Postman, is a highly vocal proponent of this specific positioning methodology, explicitly endorsing the frameworks, utilizing them within his organization, and routinely gifting the associated methodology books to peers across the industry.2

### **The Product-Led Growth Ceiling**

Postman achieved massive, unprecedented initial success through a pure Product-Led Growth (PLG) motion. The product was adopted organically by millions of individual developers worldwide who recognized its immediate, frictionless utility for building and testing APIs.19 However, a common, often fatal challenge in pure PLG models is monetizing that massive, free user base at the enterprise, organizational level.20

While individual end-users love the product for its ergonomics, capturing high-value, top-down, seven-figure contracts requires selling to a completely different persona. The enterprise buyer is typically the Chief Information Officer (CIO), the Chief Information Security Officer (CISO), or the VP of Engineering.19 These executives do not inherently care about individual developer ergonomics; they care deeply about security, data governance, compliance, and organizational efficiency.19

### **Structuring the Sales Narrative for the Enterprise Buyer**

When a successful PLG company attempts to awkwardly layer a traditional enterprise sales motion on top of their existing bottom-up motion, severe friction and deal stalling often occur.20 Sales representatives, accustomed to speaking to developers, may jump into the process too early, confusing users who just want to use the free tool. Conversely, they may attempt to pitch individual, low-level technical features to executive buyers who view those features as irrelevant to their macroscopic goals.20

The positioning workshop resolves this tension by fundamentally separating the *user* from the *buyer* and crafting a dedicated, highly targeted sales narrative specifically for the latter.20 In the case of organizations like Postman, the workshop framework helps build a narrative that leverages the existing, massive PLG footprint not merely as a user metric, but as a strategic, undeniable advantage in the enterprise sale.19

Instead of pitching the software's technical API testing features to the CIO, the sales narrative, forged through the workshop's "Market Insight" and "Differentiated Value" exercises, shifts to addressing organizational risk.9 The pitch evolves into a compelling, undeniable business case: *Your developers are already using this tool extensively in the shadows; by purchasing an enterprise license, you are not buying new software, you are gaining critical visibility, centralized security, and total governance over the infrastructure your team is already reliant upon*.19 This massive transition—from a feature-based user pitch to a risk-and-governance buyer pitch—is a direct, engineered outcome of the differentiated value exercises conducted during the positioning workshop.9

## **The Eight-Step Sales Pitch: Outputting the Narrative**

A primary deliverable of the positioning workshop, beyond the core positioning canvas, is the translation of the positioning into a highly structured sales pitch. As noted in analyses of the methodology (such as the breakdown by Pedalix and insights from the "Sales Pitch" frameworks), the workshop culminates in the creation of an eight-step storyboard.10 This structure is explicitly designed to guide the buyer, categorize their options, and teach them how to make a safe, informed decision, thereby mitigating the paralyzing fear of "messing up" a major purchase.9

| Pitch Step | Function within the Narrative (Based on Workshop Frameworks) | Mechanical Purpose |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **1\. Market Insight** | Establishes a unique, expert perspective on the current state of the industry. | Builds immense trust; proves to the buyer that the vendor deeply understands their underlying business reality.12 |
| **2\. Alternatives** | Explicitly sizes up the competition and the status quo.12 | Anchors the buyer's expectations and prevents them from silently comparing the product to the wrong category.12 |
| **3\. The Perfect World** | Paints a picture of what a flawless solution would look like, based on the market insight.12 | Establishes the exact buying criteria the prospect *should* be using, heavily favoring the vendor's strengths.12 |
| **4\. Introduction** | Formally introduces the company and the specific solution.12 | Transitions from industry theory to the concrete product offering. |
| **5\. Differentiated Value** | Unveils the specific features, tightly mapped to the "Perfect World" criteria.12 | Proves how the product uniquely solves the pain points established in steps 1-3. |
| **6\. Proof** | Provides irrefutable evidence (case studies, metrics, technical validation).12 | De-risks the purchase for the executive buyer.9 |
| **7\. Objections** | Proactively addresses known doubts, integration fears, or compliance issues.9 | Removes final friction points before they can derail the deal.9 |
| **8\. Call to Action** | The specific ask to move the process forward.12 | Initiates the procurement or evaluation phase. |

This storyboard is not a theoretical exercise; it becomes the exact script utilized by the sales team in live deals, ensuring that every representative in the organization is delivering a mathematically optimized, unified story.4

## **Modalities of Implementation: Enterprise Workshops vs. Accelerated Sprints**

The implementation of these positioning methodologies varies significantly depending on the scale, budget, and operational constraints of the organization. Participant data and industry service listings reveal two primary modalities for experiencing these workshops: the high-touch, multi-day executive alignment workshop, and the highly structured, cohort-based sprint.

### **The Premium Executive Alignment Workshop**

For mid-market and enterprise B2B SaaS companies, the standard implementation is a fully facilitated, three-day executive workshop.3 Priced transparently between $80,000 and $100,000, this format requires premium budget allocation and the dedicated, uninterrupted time of the entire senior executive suite.3

The primary advantage of this format, as explicitly noted by participants like Skillsoft and Funnel, is the real-time, hands-on facilitation provided directly by the positioning expert.3 The facilitator actively manages the complex, highly charged interpersonal dynamics of the executive team. They act as a neutral third party who can confidently break ties, fiercely challenge long-held internal biases regarding product superiority, and force consensus when discussions stall.22 The deliverable from this highly bespoke, high-intensity engagement is a fully realized, differentiated positioning strategy and a finalized, sales-ready messaging narrative that the entire go-to-market team can deploy immediately to reset complex deal motions.21

### **The Structured Product Positioning Sprint**

For earlier-stage startups, product managers, and marketing professionals who require the methodology but cannot justify or allocate enterprise-level consulting budgets, the workshop experience has been adapted into intensive, multi-week sprint formats. This includes educational programs previously offered in partnership with platforms like Section4, as well as specialized operational sprints like those offered by Mojo PMM.15

Priced at a more accessible range (e.g., $750 to $1,000 for educational cohorts), these sprints provide a rigorous "crash course in strategic product positioning".23 The curriculum relies on a blend of asynchronous, on-demand video modules and interactive live sessions.23 Participants gain hands-on experience through the dissection of new, complex case studies derived from high-profile consulting engagements—such as those involving Help Scout and Postman—and are equipped with the specific tools, templates, and agendas needed to lead the positioning exercise internally within their own organizations.23

Similarly, third-party implementations like the Mojo PMM 2-week messaging sprint utilize the foundational methodology to solve the common challenge of achieving stakeholder buy-in.15 Rather than spending weeks in scattered Google Docs and endless, unproductive Slack threads, teams work through a structured process combining live internal workshops and asynchronous homework.15 While these sprint formats lack the bespoke, in-room executive facilitation of the premium $100k workshop, they successfully empower internal champions to act as the facilitator, guiding their own cross-functional teams through the crucial steps of customer insight gathering, competitive analysis, and category selection.15

## **Third-Order Implications for B2B SaaS Organizations**

The true, lasting value of a positioning workshop extends far beyond the immediate, tactical creation of a new sales deck or a website redesign. Comprehensive participant testimonies indicate that the exercise triggers a cascade of third-order operational and strategic benefits that fundamentally alter how the company operates, allocates capital, and drives growth in the long term.

### **Mitigating the Risk of the Status Quo**

In complex B2B enterprise sales, the fiercest competitor is rarely another software vendor; it is the customer's inherent, powerful bias toward inaction. Executive buyers are terrified of making a career-limiting mistake by purchasing the wrong enterprise software, integrating it poorly, and disrupting operations.9 Because considered purchases are intensely difficult and impact many downstream stakeholders, customers are significantly less worried about missing out on a shiny new feature than they are about severely messing up an implementation.9

The workshop methodology explicitly and meticulously addresses this buyer psychology. It completely shifts the sales team's posture from aggressively "selling a product" to consultatively "helping the customer buy".9 By clearly categorizing the market options and providing the buyer with a distinct, objective framework for making a choice—based on the unique market insight developed during the first step of the workshop—the sales representative transitions from a peddling vendor to a highly trusted industry guide.9 This structured narrative directly combats the customer's fear of failure, drastically reducing the percentage of deals lost to "no decision" or the safety of the status quo.24

### **The Compound Effect on Organizational Velocity**

When a company lacks clear, documented positioning, every department is forced to expend massive amounts of wasted cognitive energy attempting to translate the product's value for their specific, siloed needs. The marketing team struggles to write high-converting copy, the sales team struggles to handle routine objections without escalating to engineering, and the product team struggles to prioritize the feature roadmap without a clear guiding star.26 This constant, repetitive translation causes severe, hidden organizational drag.

The workshop permanently eliminates this internal friction by establishing a central, immutable source of truth regarding the product's exact value and its optimal target audience.4 As comprehensively noted in the Ventup case study, once the intellectual hurdle of the new positioning framework is overcome, a massive "compound effect" takes hold across the organization.4 Because Marketing, Sales, Customer Success, and Product are operating from the exact same strategic baseline, collateral generation accelerates exponentially, cross-departmental reviews become highly streamlined, and strategic alignment is maintained effortlessly without constant executive intervention.4

### **Roadmap Prioritization and Long-Term Product Strategy**

Finally, the positioning workshop exerts a heavy, defining influence on future product development and engineering capital allocation.26 By defining precisely why the best customers currently love the product, and explicitly stating the competitive category in which the product operates, the product team gains a crystal-clear, objective lens for prioritizing the engineering roadmap.26

Feature requests that do not actively reinforce the newly defined differentiated value, or that do not serve the newly narrowed customer segment, are easily and confidently discarded.27 Conversely, specific features that deepen the competitive moat within the chosen market category receive accelerated funding and immediate engineering attention. The roadmap ceases to be a disparate, reactionary collection of loud customer requests or theoretical engineering explorations; it becomes a precise strategic weapon meticulously designed to validate, enforce, and expand the company's established positioning in the market.26
