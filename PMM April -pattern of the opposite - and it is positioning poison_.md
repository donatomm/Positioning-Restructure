[Marketers often worry the company is overly optimistic about its ability to win in the market. Interestingly, I\'ve seen a pattern of the opposite - and it is positioning poison.]{.mark}

[Don\'t get me wrong - I\'ve seen some companies that have been strong market leaders in the past be blind to competitors that have surpassed them - but I\'m surprised how often I see the exact opposite. These are growing companies with tens of millions in revenue (sometimes hundreds) with teams that are so down on their own product that it\'s hard to get them to admit there is any situation where they could win business. Yet they win business every day!]{.mark}

[I suspect there are a set of root causes:]{.mark}

[1️⃣ An overly-broad definition of an ideal prospect - this leaves the team feeling that every lost deal is proof that the product sucks, rather than our failure to disqualify obviously bad-fit prospects early in the sales cycle.]{.mark}

[2️⃣ Treating every company that looks like us as a \"competitor\" - we can all Google a fantasy competitive list, but it is impossible to differentiate against a list that long. Thankfully, we don\'t have to. For the purposes of positioning, we only need to worry about what a prospect considers as an alternative. Sales know this list - we should believe them when they tell us.]{.mark}

[3️⃣ Nobody listens to sales - hey I get it - sales has a reputation for pushing the product team to build features that wouldn\'t really close \"that one big deal we lost,\" and marketing is also sick of sales complaining about lead quality. But nobody in the company spends as much time with customers as sales. We need to develop our skills for pulling information from the sales team. Customer research might get us some of the answers but it can\'t be our only source of customer knowledge.]{.mark}

[4️⃣ The product team sees themselves as problem identifiers and not value architects - I listened to Lenny\'s conversation with Marty Cagan this week and wondered if the root cause of product pessimism relates to his idea of the product team being a \"feature factory\" rather than a true product team. I have no idea but I do know that these companies lack a clear point of view on the market and a vision that is helping the team understand where they win today and in the future.]{.mark}
