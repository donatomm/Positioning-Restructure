### **Introduction Summary**

Sara Santanen, a Positioning Consultant at FletchPMM, initiated a discussion on a common but destructive issue in B2B startups: the premature demand for \"data-driven validation\" of a new positioning strategy. She argues that true validation for a sales-led B2B company can take up to two years, and the immediate call for A/B testing or other short-term metrics is often a sign of a lack of conviction rather than a commitment to data. This disguised skepticism can derail the entire go-to-market effort, leading to a \"rigged game\" of chasing unattainable proof and ultimately burning out the team. The thread explores the tension between the long-term commitment required for successful positioning and the startup world\'s pressure for rapid, measurable results.

### **Takeaway Summary**

**Top Themes:**

- **The Long Road to Validation:** The central theme is that validating a new positioning strategy in a sales-led B2B context is a long-term endeavor, estimated at around two years. This involves updating all collateral, running campaigns, filling the pipeline, and tracking revenue KPIs over an extended period.

- **Conviction vs. \"Data-Driven\" Paralysis:** A key point of discussion is the difference between genuine conviction in a strategic decision and using the guise of being \"data-driven\" to mask a lack of buy-in. This lack of conviction leads to endless cycles of testing and analysis-paralysis, preventing the new positioning from ever being fully implemented.

- **Startup Impatience:** Several commenters highlight the friction between this long-term strategic view and the typical startup culture, where founders and teams expect to see results in weeks, not years. This impatience often leads to premature abandonment of a sound strategy.

- **The Need for Leading Indicators:** A conflicting theme emerges from participants who argue that waiting two years for feedback is unrealistic. They advocate for identifying and tracking leading indicators and shorter-term metrics (within 3-6 months) to ensure the strategy is on the right path, blending long-term vision with shorter feedback loops.

### Consensus Points:

There is a strong consensus among positioning consultants, copywriters, and experienced marketers that positioning is a long-term commitment requiring disciplined execution and conviction from leadership. They agree that founders often get impatient and that the demand for immediate \"proof\" is a common and destructive pattern. This group, including Jon McGreevy, Amanda Petersen, and Anthony Pierri, understands that constant second-guessing prevents a strategy from ever gaining market traction.

### Conflicting Takes:

The primary conflict revolves around the practicality of the two-year timeline. Commenters like Angel Umez, Kranthi Kiran, and Mike Hook push back, arguing that fast-moving startups cannot afford to wait that long without any data. They challenge the premise that there are no reliable short-term feedback mechanisms, suggesting that tools for message resonance testing or defining leading indicators at each milestone are necessary to de-risk the process. This group represents a more agile, execution-focused perspective that demands shorter feedback loops to justify continued investment in a strategy.

One sneaky thing destroys positioning exercises.

And it disguises itself well.

It's not out of malice or ill will\...

But if left unchecked, it will burn out your team and resources -- with nothing to show for it.

![](media/image1.png){width="6.5in" height="8.125in"}👇👇👇👇👇👇

Startups typically come to us with clear positioning problems:

❌ People read the website, but don't really get it

❌ They get it, but not why they should pick them

❌ They need to "educate the market" so they buy

❌ They have too many segments and can't prioritise

❌ They have too many products and can\'t explain the suite

So\...

→ We run the whole project.

→ We address the problems.

→ We define the positioning.

The team seems excited.

But then, out of the blue 😰

This talk begins:

→ \"Now we need some validation"

→ "We could do some A/B-testing\"

→ \"Let\'s test to see if the positioning works\"

🚩🚩🚩🚩🚩🚩🚩🚩🚩

Here's why that's a big fat red flag.

It is possible to test the positioning...

But for sales-led B2B startup, it just takes about two years:

→ Updating all sales & marketing collateral (Months 1-3)

→ Run marketing campaigns (Months 3-6+)

→ Fill your pipeline with ICP leads (Months 6-9)

→ Pitching ICP customers new messaging (Month 6-12)

→ Track leads & revenue KPIs over 12-24 months

→ Finally try to evaluate if positioning is "working" (after \~2 years)

Being data driven is great.

But it's not possible in this context!

So if someone's knee-jerk reaction is "testing & validation"\...

That's usually a sign of a lack of conviction.

Just disguised as being "data-driven."

And fair enough.

Maybe they're actually not totally bought in with the decisions that were made.

But for the team, that can be deadly.

See, what tends to happen is that the "testing the positioning" turns into a rigged game between those who believe in it, and those they can never convince.

That lack of conviction will come out as the devil's advocate, and endless fuzzy expectations of indisputable proof that cannot be met.

So if you're second-guessing the positioning, be honest about it both to yourself, and your team.

Figure out if there is something realistic that could turn you into a believer, or if you actually need to go back to the drawing board.

But don't say it's about having a "data-driven approach" to testing or validation, because that will simply lead the entire team astray, chasing something that cannot be achieved.
