### Example 1: The \"We Do Everything for Everyone\" Database

Remember my database story from the resources? Here\'s how to anti-position it:

**\"We\'re a revolutionary data solution that\'s simultaneously a database, a BI tool, an ETL platform, a data lake, a reporting engine, and a coffee maker. *Why it\'s terrible:* Nobody knows what the hell you actually do well.

### Example 2: The \"Legacy is the New Innovation\" CRM

Taking a page from the positioning jujitsu doc, but backwards:

**\"Introducing CRM-osaurus: The only CRM that proudly requires 6 months of implementation, a team of 12 consultants, and a blood sacrifice to your IT department. Why adapt to modern workflows when you can force your team to adapt to our 1997 interface? Now with 30% more dropdown menus!\"**

*Why it\'s terrible:* You\'re positioning your weaknesses as strengths to people who hate those exact things.

### Example 3: The \"Mystery Box\" SaaS

Violating every principle from Anthony Pierri\'s framework:

**\"We\'re building something incredible. What is it? That\'s for us to know and you to find out after a 14-step sales process. Our target market? Anyone with a computer. Our differentiation? We\'ll tell you after you sign the contract. Trust us, it\'s disruptive!\"**

*Why it\'s terrible:* Zero clarity on use case, segment, or value. It\'s positioning via riddle.

### Example 4: The \"Competitive Confusion\" Approach

Instead of choosing a clear competitive alternative:

**\"We\'re like Slack meets Excel meets Uber, but for B2B procurement. Or maybe we\'re disrupting email? Actually, we\'re replacing Post-it notes. Wait, no - we\'re the Peloton of project management!\"**

*Why it\'s terrible:* Your prospects\' brains just melted trying to figure out what category you\'re in.

### Example 5: The \"Technical Jargon Tornado\"

For the product illiteracy crowd:

**\"Our AI-powered, blockchain-enabled, quantum-ready platform leverages synergistic paradigms to facilitate cross-functional ideation matrices through our proprietary neural-symbolic architecture. ROI? That\'s so Web 2.0!\"**

*Why it\'s terrible:* You sound like a random buzzword generator had a baby with a LinkedIn influencer.

### The Anti-Positioning Canvas

Let me flip Anthony Pierri\'s positioning canvas on its head:

- **Use Case**: \"Doing\... things? With data? Or people? Actually, whatever you want!\"

- **Target Segment**: \"Companies that exist\"

- **Competitive Alternative**: \"Everything and nothing\"

- **Problem with Alternative**: \"They\'re too focused and clear about what they do\"

- **How You Do It Differently**: \"We don\'t\"

- **Why It\'s Better**: \"It\'s not, but we have a cool logo\"

### The Ultimate Anti-Positioning Statement

Following the dreaded Mad Libs format April Dunford hates:

*\"For \[anyone with money\] who \[does things\], our product is a \[noun\] that \[verbs\]. Unlike \[all competitors and non-competitors\], we \[exist\].\"*

Here\'s the thing - this experiment is brilliant because it shows exactly why good positioning matters. Every anti-positioning example makes me cringe because I\'ve seen real companies do versions of these things. They\'re trying so hard not to exclude anyone that they end up appealing to no one.

The lesson? If your positioning could work for literally any product in any market, you\'re doing it wrong. If a prospect can\'t figure out in 10 seconds whether you\'re relevant to them, you\'re doing it wrong. If you\'re afraid to pick an enemy or choose a specific use case because you might \"limit your market,\" you\'re doing it wrong.

## Zendesk Anti-Positioning: A Systematic Destruction

**Original (Good) Positioning:** \"Zendesk is a service-first CRM company that builds software designed to improve customer relationships. Our software is powerful and flexible, with an omnichannel solution that puts email, chat, social, and voice in the same place, so your business can focus on the conversation and deliver personalised experience to your customers.\"

### 1. Anti-Position the Market Category

**Original:** \"service-first CRM company\"

**Anti-positioned:**

- \"We\'re a CRM\... or maybe a help desk\... actually we\'re a ticketing system\... wait, no, we\'re a knowledge base\... honestly, we\'re whatever you want us to be!\"

- \"We operate in the customer-service-support-success-experience-engagement-satisfaction-delight space\"

- \"We\'re not NOT a CRM, but we\'re also not NOT not a CRM\"

### 2. Anti-Position the Target Customer

**Original:** Clear B2B focus - \"your business\"

**Anti-positioned:**

- \"Perfect for businesses, individuals, non-profits, governments, alien civilizations, and sentient houseplants\"

- \"Whether you have 0 customers or 10 billion customers, we\'re equally mediocre for both!\"

- \"Ideal for companies who have customers\... or might have customers someday\... or are thinking about having customers\"

### 3. Anti-Position the Problem/Need

**Original:** \"improve customer relationships\"

**Anti-positioned:**

- \"We solve the problem of\... having problems?\"

- \"For when you need to do things with stuff related to people who buy things\"

- \"Addressing the critical challenge of needing software that does software things\"

### 4. Anti-Position the Value Proposition

**Original:** \"focus on the conversation and deliver personalised experience\"

**Anti-positioned:**

- \"We enable you to achieve outcomes through activities that result in results\"

- \"Deliver experiences that are experiential to customers who customer\"

- \"Create value by adding value to your valuable value chain of value\"

### 5. Anti-Position the Differentiation

**Original:** \"powerful and flexible, omnichannel solution\"

**Anti-positioned:**

- \"We\'re exactly like our competitors, but different in ways we can\'t articulate!\"

- \"Our unique differentiator is that we have no unique differentiators\"

- \"Unlike other solutions that put things in one place, we also put things in one place, but it\'s OUR place\"

### 6. Anti-Position the Features/Capabilities

**Original:** \"puts email, chat, social, and voice in the same place\"

**Anti-positioned:**

- \"We support every communication channel ever invented including carrier pigeon, smoke signals, and interpretive dance\"

- \"Our platform does things. Many things. All the things. Things you haven\'t even thought of. Things that haven\'t been invented yet.\"

- \"Features include: features, functionality, capabilities, and other feature-like things\"

## The Ultimate Anti-Positioned Zendesk Statement:

\"Zendesk is some kind of software thing that exists in the general vicinity of customer-ish interactions. We help entities of various types do stuff with other entities through methods and means. Our solution-platform-tool-system-thingy includes things you might use to communicate about topics with beings who may or may not purchase items or services. We\'re different from others because we exist and do things differently than the way others do things, assuming others exist and do things.\"

## Bonus: Anti-Position Their Tagline

**Original:** \"Champions of customer service\"

**Anti-positioned:**

- \"Participants in activities tangentially related to service-adjacent concepts\"

- \"Somewhat involved in the general area of customer-ish stuff\"

- \"Present in the arena where service things might occur\"

This systematic destruction shows how every single element of good positioning can be ruined by:

- Being vague instead of specific

- Trying to appeal to everyone

- Avoiding concrete claims

- Using meaningless buzzwords

- Failing to articulate clear value

- Being afraid to pick a lane

The result? A company that sounds like it was positioned by a committee of people who were afraid to make any actual decisions. Pure positioning poison!

## Zendesk Anti-Positioning: Analytical Reversal

**2. Specific Purpose: \"improve customer relationships\"**

- Strength: Clear, singular focus

- Reversed: \"Zendesk helps manage customer data, track sales, handle support tickets, analyze metrics, and coordinate teams\"

**3. Defined Capabilities: \"email, chat, social, and voice in the same place\"**

- Strength: Concrete channels, clear integration benefit

- Reversed: \"Zendesk offers multiple communication options\"

- Why it\'s weaker: Vague, doesn\'t explain the integration value

**4. Clear Benefit: \"focus on the conversation\"**

- Strength: Human-centered, differentiating approach

- Reversed: \"Zendesk helps you handle customer inquiries efficiently\"

- Why it\'s weaker: Efficiency is table stakes, not differentiation

**5. Customer Outcome: \"deliver personalised experience\"**

- Strength: Specific, valuable end result

- Reversed: \"Zendesk helps you serve customers\"

- Why it\'s weaker: Generic outcome every service software claims

## The Complete Analytically Reversed Positioning:

\"Zendesk is a CRM company that also handles service. We offer multiple communication options to help you manage customer data, track sales, handle support tickets, analyze metrics, and coordinate teams. Our software helps you handle customer inquiries efficiently so you can serve customers.\"

### Why This Reversal Destroys Value:

1.  **Lost Focus**: Instead of \"service-first,\" it\'s now everything-equal

2.  **No Priority**: Five different jobs instead of one clear mission

3.  **Generic Benefits**: \"Serve customers\" vs \"personalised experience\"

4.  **No Integration Story**: \"Multiple options\" vs \"in the same place\"

5.  **No Philosophy**: Just features, no underlying belief system

This analytical reversal shows how strong positioning isn\'t about fancy words - it\'s about making clear choices. When you reverse those choices, you get positioning that\'s technically accurate but strategically worthless.

## How Companies Drift from Strong to Weak Positioning

Based on what we\'ve seen in our analysis and the positioning resources we\'ve reviewed, here are the patterns of drift:

### 1. Growth Pressure → \"We Need a Bigger Market\"

When companies face growth pressure, there\'s temptation to broaden positioning. Using our analyzed examples:

- Payhip\'s \"for creators\" could drift to \"for anyone selling online\"

- Airhouse\'s \"direct-to-consumer companies\" could expand to \"all e-commerce\"

The resources from April Dunford warn about this explicitly - trying to be everything to everyone makes you nothing to no one.

### 2. Feature Creep → \"We Do More Now\"

HubSpot is a documented example of this evolution: \"While it started as an internet marketing software, its positioning shifted to being an inbound marketing software, and now a growth platform.\" [[A Deep Dive into HubSpot for Startups \| A Complete Guide]{.underline}](https://www.3andfour.com/articles/hubspot-for-startups)

The challenge is that as products add features, the positioning can become less clear. \"Growth platform\" is broader and vaguer than \"inbound marketing software.\"

### 3. Competitive Pressure → \"We Need to Match Them\"

When competitors make claims, companies feel pressure to match. If everyone claims to be \"omnichannel\" or \"AI-powered,\" companies add these terms even if they dilute the core positioning.

### 4. Internal Politics → \"Everyone Needs Representation\"

As teams grow, each department wants their work reflected in positioning. Sales wants sales features mentioned, support wants service capabilities highlighted. The positioning statement becomes a feature list trying to make everyone internally happy.

### 5. Customer Success Stories → \"Look Who Else We Serve\"

You land one Fortune 500 client as a startup focused on SMBs, and suddenly you\'re tempted to say \"from startups to Fortune 500.\" But this dilutes the focus that made you successful with SMBs in the first place.

### 6. Fear of Missing Out → \"We Should Address That Market Too\"

Seeing competitors or adjacent companies succeed in other segments creates FOMO. A D2C fulfillment company sees B2B fulfillment growth and wants in, even though it requires different capabilities, sales motions, and operational models.

### The Telltale Signs of Drift:

1.  **Adding \"and\" repeatedly**: \"We serve creators AND businesses AND enterprises\"

2.  **Qualifier creep**: \"We\'re a CRM\" becomes \"We\'re a CRM platform\" becomes \"We\'re a customer engagement platform\"

3.  **Vague superlatives**: Moving from specific claims to \"best-in-class,\" \"leading,\" \"comprehensive\"

4.  **Lost origination story**: Can no longer explain why the company was started

5.  **Sales confusion**: Reps create their own positioning because the official one doesn\'t help them

The key insight from our analytical reversals: drift happens one small compromise at a time. Each change seems reasonable in isolation, but collectively they transform clear positioning into generic mush.
