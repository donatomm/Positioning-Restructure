# **A Critical Evaluation of April Dunford\'s \"Obviously Awesome\" Approach to Product Positioning**

***Summary**: April Dunford\'s \"Obviously Awesome\" offers a 10-step framework for product positioning, emphasizing understanding customer needs, competitive alternatives, and unique value. It\'s praised for its practical approach and actionable advice. However, criticisms include its applicability to established brands versus startups, and its stance against traditional positioning statements. Dunford stresses \"competitive alternatives\" beyond direct competitors, including the \"status quo.\" Other positioning frameworks like Trout\'s Differentiation, Blue Ocean Strategy, and Jobs to Be Done offer different perspectives. Implementing Dunford\'s methods can be challenging due to identifying \"best-fit\" customers, overcoming \"positioning baggage,\" and balancing narrow focus with broader objectives. The framework requires ongoing adaptation in dynamic markets and in-depth customer engagement. Clarifications are needed regarding \"Market Category,\" \"Relevant Trends,\" measuring success, positioning novel products, and the interplay between positioning and branding. Overall, \"Obviously Awesome\" is a valuable guide for creating customer-centric positioning strategies, though it may require adjustments based on specific contexts.*

**1. Introduction: Deconstructing \"Obviously Awesome\" - Core Principles of April Dunford\'s Positioning**

April Dunford\'s \"Obviously Awesome\" presents a compelling argument that effective product positioning transcends the creation of clever taglines or superficial marketing strategies, instead forming the very bedrock upon which customers perceive a product and ultimately make purchasing decisions ^1^. Dunford challenges conventional, often outdated positioning templates and practices, which she argues are frequently formulaic and fail to yield meaningful results ^1^. In their place, she introduces a contemporary approach characterized by its dynamic and iterative nature, deeply embedded in a thorough understanding of customer needs and the broader market context ^1^. This perspective underscores a fundamental shift in how positioning should be approached, moving away from a mere exercise in crafting marketing slogans to a more profound engagement with market realities and customer understanding.

The challenge of achieving effective product positioning is particularly acute within the technology sector, often proving elusive even for seasoned marketers ^2^. For technology founders, positioning can feel like an unpredictable art, with a general understanding of its importance but a persistent difficulty in its execution ^2^. The traditional marketing pursuit of product-market fit has often commenced with the formulation of a \"positioning statement\" ^2^. However, this conventional approach frequently falls short because it lacks the crucial details on how the intended positioning would be implemented in practice ^2^. Consequently, the task of positioning is sometimes relegated to junior marketers, briefly discussed in meetings, and then overlooked as just another superficial exercise, a phenomenon Dunford herself experienced early in her career ^2^. This highlights a significant gap that Dunford\'s work seeks to address: the transition from abstract positioning concepts to concrete, actionable strategies.

In response to these challenges, \"Obviously Awesome\" offers a structured ten-step process designed to guide businesses toward successful product positioning, enriched with practical, real-world examples of companies that have successfully transformed their market standing through astute positioning decisions ^2^. The book has been described as a practical guide, akin to a \"manual for positioning,\" complete with visual aids like infographics and charts to illustrate its concepts ^4^. Dunford emphasizes the critical importance of a thorough and repeated examination of the market environment to arrive at a winning product placement strategy and its effective execution ^4^. This iterative analytical approach is a key differentiator of \"Obviously Awesome,\" as it extends beyond simply describing the theoretical landscape of positioning to providing a clear roadmap for practitioners to achieve their desired outcomes ^2^.

At the core of Dunford\'s methodology are five essential components, with a bonus element, that collectively define effective positioning: Competitive Alternatives, Unique Attributes, Value, Target Market Characteristics, Market Category, and Relevant Trends ^5^. These components are not isolated entities but rather are deeply interconnected and interdependent ^2^. For instance, the unique value a product offers is contingent upon its differentiated features, which in turn are only truly differentiated when compared against competitive alternatives ^6^. Similarly, the most suitable target customers are those who highly value the unique benefits the product provides ^6^. This interconnectedness underscores the necessity of considering these components holistically rather than in isolation to achieve a coherent and impactful positioning strategy.

**2. Acknowledging the Acclaim: Positive Reception and Impact of Dunford\'s Work**

April Dunford\'s \"Obviously Awesome\" has been met with widespread acclaim from marketing professionals across various industries. The book has been lauded as \"obviously awesome\" and has proven to be an invaluable resource for individuals and teams working in product development, sales, and marketing ^4^. Many readers, particularly those in startup environments grappling with the complexities of positioning, have found it to be a highly impactful and insightful read ^7^. The content is frequently described as exceptionally actionable, providing a clear bias towards practical implementation rather than just theoretical discussion ^7^. Furthermore, the book\'s approach is often praised for its clarity and straightforwardness, making a potentially daunting topic accessible and understandable ^7^.

The value of Dunford\'s expertise and the practical utility of her book are further underscored by endorsements from numerous industry leaders. Rich Mironov, a respected CEO and product management expert, unequivocally identifies Dunford as the \"master at positioning.\" He commends \"Obviously Awesome\" for its ability to demonstrate *how* to effectively position products, moving beyond mere theoretical discussions to provide concrete guidance ^8^. Kirk Simpson, the CEO of Wave, considers the book an essential read for anyone struggling to differentiate their product in competitive markets ^8^. Simon Galbraith, CEO of Red Gate Software, highlights the tangible impact of Dunford\'s positioning work on his own company\'s substantial revenue growth, recommending her book as an accessible framework for others to achieve similar results ^8^. Mark Organ, the founder and CEO of Influitive, specifically recommends \"Obviously Awesome\" for products that customers find difficult to understand, emphasizing its power in clarifying a product\'s value proposition ^8^. These endorsements from prominent figures within the technology and marketing spheres serve as strong validation of Dunford\'s methodology and its real-world effectiveness.

A significant strength of \"Obviously Awesome\" lies in its ability to demystify the often-complex world of product positioning and provide readers with tangible, implementable steps. Customers consistently appreciate the book\'s writing style, which is characterized by its clarity, conciseness, and engaging tone ^7^. The language is direct and avoids unnecessary jargon, allowing readers to quickly grasp the core concepts and apply them to their own situations ^7^. Many have noted that the book effectively \"lifts the veil of positioning,\" revealing its power and making it accessible even to those who previously found it an abstract or intimidating topic ^7^. This focus on clarity and actionability is a key reason why \"Obviously Awesome\" has resonated so strongly with marketing practitioners seeking practical solutions to their positioning challenges.

**3. Challenging the Narrative: Criticisms and Limitations of Dunford\'s Positioning Framework**

While April Dunford\'s \"Obviously Awesome\" has garnered significant praise, some marketing professionals have raised questions regarding the universal applicability and specific aspects of her framework. One area of discussion revolves around the effectiveness of her approach in different market contexts, particularly when comparing its use for established brands versus new products or startups. A user on Reddit suggested that the \"context\" component of Dunford\'s framework, which is considered crucial, might be more readily applicable to truly novel offerings and early-stage companies ^9^. The argument is that for established brands operating in mature markets, it can be more challenging to envision and implement entirely new market contexts because customers already possess a well-formed understanding of the existing product category ^9^. In such scenarios, customers are more likely to evaluate products based on direct comparisons of features and specifications within their existing mental model of the market ^9^. This suggests that while Dunford\'s emphasis on context setting is valuable, its implementation might require a different approach for established players compared to disruptors or those introducing entirely new product categories.

For established brands, an alternative strategy might involve a greater focus on incremental innovation and positioning based on specific feature differentiation within the already understood market context ^9^. Instead of attempting to redefine the entire market landscape, these brands might find more success in highlighting unique features or superior performance compared to direct competitors within the existing category. This approach acknowledges the established customer understanding and seeks to carve out a distinct position based on tangible product advantages rather than a re-framing of the entire market.

Another significant point of contention, or at least a clear divergence from traditional marketing thought, is Dunford\'s strong critique of the conventional \"positioning statement\" exercise ^3^. She argues that this exercise, often involving filling in a template to create a concise statement of a product\'s position relative to competitors, is largely ineffective and potentially detrimental ^3^. Dunford contends that the positioning statement exercise operates under the flawed assumption that effective positioning is already known and simply needs to be articulated in a specific format ^3^. In reality, she posits that a product can be positioned in numerous different ways, each with significant implications for the business. The exercise, by suggesting a singular \"correct\" answer, discourages the exploration of these various possibilities ^3^. Furthermore, Dunford argues that the output of this exercise is often an awkward and convoluted statement that is difficult for teams to understand, remember, or effectively use ^3^. Finally, she believes that creating a positioning statement can create a false sense of accomplishment, leading companies to believe that positioning is a one-time task rather than an ongoing process that needs to adapt to market changes ^3^. This perspective challenges a widely accepted marketing practice and suggests that Dunford\'s framework prioritizes a more dynamic and nuanced understanding of positioning over a static, written declaration.

While Dunford firmly opposes traditional positioning statements, it\'s worth noting that other marketing frameworks continue to utilize them, often with attempts to improve their utility ^11^. These frameworks might emphasize different aspects or offer variations on the template, suggesting that while the traditional approach has its flaws, the underlying concept of a concise articulation of positioning might still hold value for some organizations. Given Dunford\'s strong critique, it raises the question of what specific alternative methods she recommends for capturing and disseminating the core essence of a product\'s positioning effectively across an organization. While Step 10 of her framework mentions capturing positioning for sharing ^5^, further detail on these methods would provide a more complete understanding of her recommended approach.

A central tenet of Dunford\'s framework is the emphasis on considering \"competitive alternatives\" rather than solely focusing on direct competitors ^13^. She argues that to truly understand a product\'s position in the market, it\'s crucial to identify what customers would do if the product didn\'t exist ^13^. Often, she contends, the primary competitive alternative is the \"status quo\" -- the existing methods customers use to solve the problem, which might include spreadsheets, manual processes, or even doing nothing ^13^. By framing competition in this broader sense, Dunford forces companies to consider the full spectrum of choices available to their target customers, including the option of not adopting a new solution at all ^13^. This perspective broadens the scope of competitive analysis beyond just similar products, compelling companies to articulate their value proposition in a way that is clearly superior to all available alternatives, including the comfort and familiarity of existing solutions. This focus on \"competitive alternatives\" is a key element of Dunford\'s methodology, serving as the starting point for understanding what truly differentiates a product in the eyes of potential customers ^13^.

While the emphasis on competitive alternatives is undoubtedly valuable in prompting a more comprehensive understanding of the market landscape, a potential limitation might arise if this focus inadvertently overshadows the importance of proactively identifying and addressing emerging customer needs and evolving market dynamics that are not necessarily being met by current solutions, whether direct competitors or the status quo. By primarily focusing on existing alternatives, there\'s a risk of overlooking entirely new positioning opportunities that could arise from anticipating future customer needs or identifying unmet desires that current solutions do not address. Therefore, it would be beneficial to understand how Dunford\'s framework guides marketers in balancing the critical focus on \"competitive alternatives\" with the need to proactively identify and address emerging, unarticulated customer needs that might not be adequately served by the current range of solutions.

**4. The Landscape of Alternatives: Contrasting Dunford\'s Approach with Other Positioning Frameworks**

Beyond April Dunford\'s methodology, a diverse range of positioning frameworks exists, each offering a unique perspective on how to establish a product\'s place in the market. Jack Trout\'s differentiation strategy, for example, places a strong emphasis on creating a unique value proposition that distinguishes a brand from its competitors ^14^. This framework centers on identifying and effectively communicating what makes a brand special, whether it\'s through innovative offerings, superior quality, or a groundbreaking business model ^14^. While Dunford also highlights the importance of \"Unique Attributes,\" Trout\'s approach appears to be more directly focused on establishing clear points of difference against existing competitors, whereas Dunford\'s framework considers a broader competitive landscape that includes the status quo.

The Blue Ocean Strategy offers another distinct approach, encouraging brands to seek out uncontested market spaces, or \"blue oceans,\" where competition is essentially irrelevant ^14^. This strategy prioritizes innovation and the identification of unmet needs to create entirely new market categories or redefine existing ones ^14^. It involves a structured process of analyzing industry factors and deciding which to eliminate, reduce, raise, and create to establish a unique market position ^15^. While Dunford\'s framework includes \"Market Category\" as a key component, the Blue Ocean Strategy places a greater emphasis on fundamentally reshaping market boundaries through radical innovation, whereas Dunford\'s approach can be applied to positioning within existing market structures as well.

The Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) framework shifts the focus from product features and competitor analysis to understanding the fundamental \"job\" that customers are trying to accomplish when they \"hire\" a product ^6^. This framework emphasizes deeply understanding the circumstances surrounding the job, the obstacles customers face, and their desired outcomes ^15^. Notably, April Dunford herself has acknowledged the influence of JTBD theory on her thinking, particularly in her realization that competitive alternatives should serve as the starting point for effective positioning ^6^. While Dunford\'s framework incorporates the understanding of customer needs through the \"Value\" and \"Target Market Characteristics\" components, JTBD places the core \"job\" at the very center of the positioning effort, potentially offering a different lens through which to analyze competitive alternatives and define value.

Customer-centric positioning prioritizes the needs, preferences, and values of the customer base as the guiding principles for the positioning strategy ^14^. This approach focuses on building and maintaining customer trust by consistently delivering value that aligns with their expectations and desires ^14^. There is a clear overlap with Dunford\'s methodology, which also emphasizes understanding the \"best-fit\" customers and the value they derive from the product ^6^. However, customer-centric positioning might extend beyond the product itself to encompass the entire customer experience, whereas Dunford\'s framework is primarily focused on defining the product\'s specific place within the market relative to alternatives.

Beyond these, other frameworks offer additional perspectives. Perceptual mapping provides a visual representation of a product\'s position in the market relative to competitors based on key attributes ^15^. The Value Proposition Canvas focuses on aligning product features and benefits with specific customer needs, pains, and gains ^15^. The Positioning Diamond offers a structured approach encompassing the frame of reference, points of difference and parity, and the reason to believe a product\'s claims ^15^. The existence of this diverse array of positioning frameworks underscores the idea that the most effective approach can vary depending on the specific industry, company goals, and the particular challenges being addressed.

**5. Navigating Implementation: Challenges and Practical Considerations in Applying Dunford\'s Methods**

Implementing April Dunford\'s positioning framework, while offering a structured approach, can present several practical challenges for organizations. A key aspect of her methodology involves identifying and focusing on the \"best-fit customers\" -- those who deeply appreciate the product\'s unique value and are most likely to become enthusiastic advocates ^2^. However, in practice, pinpointing this specific segment can be difficult, especially for products that have a diverse user base with varying perceptions of value ^10^. Surveying a broad customer base might reveal a multitude of reasons why different customers find the product useful, making it challenging to discern a clear and consistent pattern of the \"best-fit\" profile ^10^.

Another significant hurdle lies in overcoming what Dunford refers to as \"positioning baggage\" ^10^. Companies often develop a deeply ingrained understanding of their product and its place in the market, sometimes rooted in the initial product idea or historical market context ^3^. This can create a resistance to seeing the product in a new light, even if the market has evolved or the product\'s capabilities have expanded beyond its original conception ^3^. Furthermore, achieving internal alignment on a new positioning strategy can be challenging, particularly when different functional groups within the organization have their own perspectives and definitions of the product and its positioning ^10^. Overcoming these ingrained assumptions and fostering a shared understanding of the desired positioning across the organization requires dedicated effort and effective communication.

Dunford advocates for a focused positioning strategy that targets a specific segment of \"best-fit\" customers to gain initial traction ^2^. However, striking the right balance between a narrowly defined focus and a sufficiently broad positioning to achieve overall business objectives can be a delicate act ^2^. While a narrow focus can help conserve resources and accelerate early adoption among the most receptive customers, making the positioning too restrictive might limit the product\'s long-term growth potential by excluding other viable customer segments ^2^. Determining the optimal scope of the positioning requires careful consideration of the company\'s growth ambitions and the potential size of the \"best-fit\" market.

In today\'s rapidly evolving markets, characterized by the swift introduction of new offerings and rapidly changing customer preferences, a static positioning strategy can quickly become outdated ^22^. While Dunford\'s 10-step process provides a structured approach, maintaining relevant and effective positioning in such dynamic environments necessitates ongoing monitoring and adaptation ^22^. The framework might need to be applied iteratively, with regular reassessments of the market landscape, competitive alternatives, and customer needs to ensure the positioning remains aligned with the current realities.

Dunford emphasizes the importance of engaging in direct conversations with customers to gain a deep understanding of their needs and the value they perceive ^23^. While this qualitative approach is invaluable for uncovering nuanced insights that might be missed by surveys alone, it can also be time-consuming and resource-intensive, particularly for companies with a large and diverse customer base. Organizations need to carefully consider the resources required for such in-depth customer engagement and balance it with other research methods.

Finally, Dunford\'s methodology often begins by analyzing the characteristics and experiences of customers who already love the product ^5^. This approach assumes the existence of a significant base of enthusiastic users. For very early-stage startups that are still searching for product-market fit and have not yet cultivated a cohort of \"best-fit\" customers, applying this initial step directly might be challenging ^10^. These companies might need to adopt a more exploratory approach to positioning initially, perhaps testing different hypotheses and iterating based on early market feedback before they have a clear set of \"raving fans\" to analyze.

**6. Unpacking Ambiguities: Questions for Deeper Understanding of Dunford\'s Concepts**

While \"Obviously Awesome\" provides a comprehensive guide to product positioning, certain aspects could benefit from further clarification to enhance a complete understanding of Dunford\'s framework. Regarding the definition of \"Market Category,\" Dunford describes it as a way of setting the context for a product ^24^. To gain a deeper understanding, it would be helpful to know how Dunford distinguishes between defining a \"market category\" and simply identifying the industry to which a product belongs. What level of specificity or granularity does she recommend for defining a market category to ensure it effectively sets the right context for potential customers?

Dunford includes \"Relevant Trends\" as a valuable, albeit bonus, component of effective positioning ^5^. Given the dynamic nature of market trends, it raises the question of how marketers should critically evaluate the long-term relevance and potential impact of a trend before integrating it into their positioning strategy. What are the potential risks or pitfalls of aligning a product\'s positioning with trends that might be fleeting or ultimately not impactful for the target customer segment ^10^?

While the book provides extensive guidance on *how* to develop a strong positioning strategy, it offers less explicit direction on how to definitively measure its success over time. Therefore, it would be valuable to understand what key performance indicators (KPIs) or specific metrics Dunford recommends for evaluating the ongoing effectiveness of a company\'s positioning strategy and tracking its impact on business outcomes.

Dunford touches upon the topic of positioning in new markets ^6^. However, for products that are truly novel or disruptive and do not fit neatly into existing, well-defined market categories, the process of establishing a relevant frame of reference for potential customers can be particularly challenging. In such cases, how does Dunford\'s framework specifically guide the process of creating a new market category or effectively positioning a groundbreaking product in a way that resonates with an audience that may not have an existing mental model for it?

Finally, Dunford makes a clear distinction between positioning and branding ^25^. To further clarify the relationship between these two crucial concepts, it would be beneficial if Dunford could elaborate on the precise interplay between a well-defined positioning strategy and the subsequent branding efforts. How does a company\'s chosen positioning inform and influence the development of its brand identity, messaging, and overall brand experience?

**7. Conclusion: A Balanced Perspective on April Dunford\'s Contribution to Product Positioning**

In conclusion, April Dunford\'s \"Obviously Awesome\" offers a valuable and highly influential framework for approaching the complex challenge of product positioning. The methodology presented is characterized by its actionability and practical guidance, providing a structured pathway for marketers to move beyond superficial positioning efforts. Dunford\'s emphasis on deeply understanding customer perception, the broader market context, and the critical role of \"competitive alternatives\" encourages a more comprehensive and strategic approach to defining a product\'s place in the market. The positive reception from a wide range of marketing professionals and endorsements from respected industry leaders further attest to the practical utility and impact of her work.

However, like any framework, Dunford\'s approach has certain limitations and areas where further consideration might be warranted. Its direct applicability might be more straightforward for new products and startups compared to established brands operating in mature markets with entrenched customer perceptions. While her critique of traditional positioning statements is thought-provoking, some marketers might still find value in a concise articulation of their product\'s position. Furthermore, the practical challenges of accurately identifying \"best-fit\" customers, overcoming internal biases, and adapting to rapidly shifting markets highlight the need for careful application and potential iteration of her methodology.

Overall, \"Obviously Awesome\" provides a significant contribution to the field of marketing strategy by demystifying product positioning and offering a customer-centric, structured process for achieving clarity and resonance in the marketplace. While certain aspects might require adaptation or further nuance depending on the specific context, Dunford\'s framework represents a substantial improvement over outdated and less effective approaches. The book serves as an essential guide for marketers seeking to develop a robust and impactful positioning strategy that ultimately drives customer understanding, purchase, and loyalty. Future research could explore the longitudinal effectiveness of Dunford\'s framework across diverse industries and market lifecycle stages, as well as investigate potential synergies between her principles and other established marketing and positioning methodologies.
