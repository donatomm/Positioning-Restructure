\<p\>So let\'s talk about how we compete against the hordes. The hordes are like everybody else in the space. Now sometimes I talk to people and they say, you know what, my space is not that crowded. I don\'t think I have this problem. And I\'m a little skeptical of that because I think most spaces are pretty crowded, at least they are from a customer\'s point of view.\</p\>

\<p\>So this chart gets shown a lot at marketing conferences. This is the marketing technology landscape a guy puts this together every year. \<h3\>Strategy 1: Fight a Fight You Can Win (Strategic Positioning)\</h3\>

\<p\>The first way we can do this is we make sure that we\'re only fighting a fight we can win. So that might sound obvious, but in general, I feel like quite often, this is particularly true of startups, we are often trying to beat Bobby Fischer at chess, if you know what I mean. Like we are running up straight against an unbeatable competitor and positioning ourselves kind of unconsciously or unintentionally in such a way that we\'re never ever going to win the way we\'re positioned.\</p\>

\<h4\>The \"Stupid\" Cake Example\</h4\>

\<p\>I\'m going to give you two examples of this, and the first one is really stupid, but I\'m hoping it illustrates the point, and then I\'m going to give you a more serious technology one. Okay, so bear with me, I\'m a stupid example. So let\'s say we\'re not in tech. What we are is we\'re cake makers, we\'re bakers. And I\'m a baker, my specialty is chocolate cake. And I wake up one morning and I\'m like, you know what I\'m going to do? I\'m going to innovate the heck out of chocolate cake because that\'s my jam. And I come up with this thing here on the right hand side, it\'s amazing. People love it. I\'ve got all my test customers are like all crazy for it. And they\'re like, here\'s my innovative cake thing, it\'s amazing. So I\'m going to launch it. And specifically, I get this idea, I\'m going to call up the gal at Starbucks that buys snack things and I\'m going to convince her that we\'re going to get Starbucks across the land should carry my amazing innovative cake thing.\</p\>

\<p\>So I call up the gal from Starbucks and here\'s how I pitch it to her. I\'m like, hey Starbucks lady, I got this thing, it\'s amazing, it\'s cake innovation. What it is, is it\'s not actually the cake, I actually took a piece of cake and then I made it kind of small, kind of like a morsel of cake. And then I decided I want to make it portable, so I gave it sort of a handle. It\'s not really a handle, it\'s more like a stick. And so I\'ve got this amazing innovative cake on a stick thing. It\'s fantastic, all my users love it, it\'s so great, you should buy some of it. And the gal at Starbucks is like, no, no, no, that is, that\'s cake doing things cake wasn\'t meant to do. Like that\'s, that\'s franken-cake is what you got there. I don\'t want that. And why does she think that? Because you set it up like a cake contest. You right out of the boat, you said it is cake. What wins a cake contest? The cakest cake in town. Like, and what do we want in cake? We want a big piece of cake. We don\'t want a little piece of cake, we want a big piece of cake. And you know what we really want? We want frosting, man, and I want tons of it. And that thing doesn\'t have frosting. And look at your product, what\'s special and different and innovative about your product? It\'s the ball and the stick and a whole bunch of stuff that has nothing to do with cake. So you set yourself up in a cake contest, you cannot win a cake contest with that thing. That thing does not win a cake contest. It is not cakier than cake. Cake wins a cake contest, you lose.\</p\>

\<p\>We could have positioned this in a different way. We could have said, hey Starbucks lady, you know what I was thinking about? I was thinking about lollipops. \<h3\>Defining Your Best Market Position\</h3\>

\<p\>So how do we find, so we\'re trying to beat the hordes, we know positioning is important, how do we actually find our best market position? Now positioning is a, is a tragically misunderstood thing. So I\'m going to start with a definition: \<em\>Positioning defines how your product is the best in the world at delivering something, some value that a well-defined set of customers cares a lot about.\</em\> That\'s a mouthful of a definition, and the reason it is, is because positioning is actually composed of five component pieces. We can think of it this way.\</p\>

\<p\>Positioning defines first of all, who\'s the competitive alternative to us? So if, if we didn\'t exist, what would folks do? Secondly, what are the unique attributes or features of our product? Next, what is the value we can deliver for customers? And then the next piece is what customers? So what is our segmentation? Who are, what\'s the definition of our best fit customers? And then lastly, market category. You know, are we a robot or a self-driving car?\</p\>

\<p\>Interestingly, when you look at these things, all of them have a relationship to each other. Like if I say, let\'s take anything on this wheel here, value. The unique value that my product delivers to customers is completely dependent on the unique features of it. But the unique features are only unique when I compare them to a competitive alternative. So those things are all related. Customer segmentation, my best fit customers, by definition, these are the folks that really, really care a lot about my value. And then market category is a little less intuitive, but if you think about it, the best market category for us to position in is the market category that helps make this value obvious to these target customers. So all these things relate to each other.\</p\>

\<h4\>Where to Start: Competitive Alternatives\</h4\>

\<p\>So how do we actually figure it out? Where\'s the starting point? I spent a lot of time thinking about this, in fact, years. And what I eventually got to was we actually have to start with competitive alternatives. Otherwise, if we start anywhere else, we\'ll come up with neat positioning, but it doesn\'t actually necessarily work out in the market because it doesn\'t position us relative to the alternative ways that people could solve the problem.\</p\>

\<p\>When most people mess this up, they mess up the first step, which is, every day I get some little startup come to me and they\'ll say, you know what, we\'re, we position our ease of use. That\'s our big thing. Like we\'re way easier to use. And here\'s our competitors, there are a whole bunch of little companies nobody ever heard of. And with them it takes 15 clicks to do a thing, and with us it only takes two. So ease of use is our thing. But then I get working with them and I\'m like, but wait a second, do you actually lose any deals to those little companies? Like they look pretty small, like some of them don\'t even look like they have any customers. Do you actually lose deals? And they\'ll say, well, no, no, we don\'t. And I\'ll say, so who do you actually compete with? Like if customers didn\'t use you, what would they do? And they\'ll say things like, oh, they probably just use a spreadsheet, or better yet, they\'ll use an intern. Like you\'re never going to beat the intern on ease of use, just never. Like, like the intern\'s super easy to use. Like, Joey, get me a coffee while you\'re at it, fill out the spreadsheet. Easy, right? So here you are walking around positioning around a competitor that isn\'t actually the real competitor.\</p\>

\<p\>Once you get that, then you can say, okay, now I know who I compete with. This is what I\'ve got that\'s unique and different from them. These are my differentiated features. Then I can say, how do those features translate to value? This is the value I can deliver for customers. Then I can say, well, what customers care a lot about that value? Those are my best fit customers. And then lastly, I\'ve got market category. I\'m trying to communicate this value to these people. What\'s the best context to put that in to do that?\</p\>

\<p\>Anyways, this is a big topic, I could talk about this for hours. I got other things to teach you today. If you want to go way down the rat hole on positioning, I wrote a whole book on it. For like the cost of a beer, you can go learn everything that there is to know about positioning that I know, at any rate. So that\'s the first bit. First, I got to make sure I\'m fighting a fight I can win by doing deliberate strategic positioning.\</p\>

\<h3\>Strategy 2: Teach Prospects How to Buy\</h3\>

\<p\>All right, how else do I beat the hordes? But this one is a little bit of a positioning thing too. What I do here is I teach my prospects how to buy. This is an interesting one that I don\'t think we think enough about, particularly in tech. Think about your product right now and think about who you sell to. Have your buyers ever purchased a thing like that before? I spent years in the CRM space, and everybody that was buying a CRM literally had never bought a CRM before. They\'d never bought one before. So how the heck do they know how to evaluate CRMs? How do they know what to pay attention to and what not to pay attention to? How do they make a short list of companies to look at?\</p\>

\<p\>Well, they Google around. Like, oh gee, let\'s have a look here. Let\'s say I\'m a buyer and I\'m thinking about buying something to help my sales team do training, sales enablement software. I\'ll look at this. Is this helpful? No, this is the opposite of helpful. Now I have 7,000 things to look for. Good, I\'m never going to look at all those things. But eventually I figure out, oh, there\'s a box in there. If I want sales enablement, that\'s what this thing is called, sales enablement. That\'s what I want. Oh, well, this is still bad. So, you know, now I don\'t have 7,000, but I still have way too many to evaluate. I can\'t look at all of these.\</p\>

\<p\>And then let\'s say eventually I land on one of these, like comparison sites, like G2 Crowd or Software Advice. Does this help me narrow it down? Well, not really, right? Look at the axes on these things. One of them is market presence. Like let\'s say I\'m a little startup, I\'m like, I look at those things at the top right and I\'m like, those look like for big companies. I\'m not a big company, I don\'t have that budget. So just because you\'re top right doesn\'t mean you\'re good for me. And here\'s the dirty secret about all these, these comparison sites: they make money off customer confusion. Think about it, how do these guys make money? They make money from you clicking around on a whole bunch of different grids going, oh my God, I don\'t know what to pay attention to here. That\'s why they have this proliferation of grids that don\'t even make sense. That\'s why they got your product in all kinds of grids that you wish your product wasn\'t even in, because the more these people click around and fill out forms, the more money they make because they sell those leads to people. This was not designed to actually help customers buy.\</p\>

\<p\>So how do customers buy? Well, how should they figure this out? Who knows a lot about how the market is split apart or who could actually teach people a sensible way to split the market or think about the market? You vendors, we\'re awesome at this. We spend all day thinking about this. We have each of us a very strong point of view about what makes a good or not a good solution in here. Now we no longer share it with customers generally because we\'re like, well, why would a customer want to hear my point of view on this? Because I\'m biased. Of course you\'re biased, customer knows your bias, but they\'re still interested in this if you can figure out a way to share your point of view on a market in an, in at least an honest way, then even though it\'s biased, it\'s still helpful.\</p\>

\<p\>Let me give you an example. So I work with this company, they\'re in the sales enablement space, right? So they\'re one of these people on here and they\'re like, this is a tragic mess and half these things we don\'t even consider half these things sales enablement. So instead what these guys did is they created a bunch of content. So one of the things they built was sales tech explained using donuts. Yeah, one of them\'s me, but here\'s two other ones that happen to be, you know, big competitive folks in that space. \<em\>Having the guts to do that gets you on a lot of short lists because that\'s all people are trying to figure out how do I get on a short list.\</em\>\</p\>

\<p\>They, they tried to, they started getting a lot of questions about, well, wait, I understand that donut thing, but how do like, how do I know where how everything interacts with everything else? So then they built another, another graphic and it was kind of like, um, it was like the shopping mall and everywhere the map of the shopping mall. \<p\>So that\'s it. This is how you stand out amongst the hordes: deliberate positioning and don\'t be afraid to just position everybody else and not just yourself.\</p\>

\<h2\>Competing Against the Giant\</h2\>

\<p\>How am I going to beat the giant? Here\'s kind of the dirty secret about this, and again, like little companies often don\'t think about this: you can position your competitors just like you position yourself, even the big ones. And so one thing, one way to do that is to kind of take their strengths and use it against them. So \<em\>inside every strength of a big, big competitor is a weakness\</em\>.\</p\>

\<h3\>Strategy 1: Use Their Strengths Against Them\</h3\>

\<p\>So I used to work at IBM and one of the things we used to talk about all the time was how proven our solutions were. We\'re like, they\'re so proven, they\'ve been around for ages, you know, whether this is mature software, you know, all the kinks are worked out of it, lots and lots of people using it. And little startups would come in and win deals against us. They just position us as legacy. Oh, that used to make us so mad. Like that\'s the old stuff. When you say your product is market leading, that\'s another way of saying it\'s unspecialized. It\'s the generic thing, it\'s the thing for everybody. Leads the market, right? Not for these people, not for these people, not for these people. If you\'re in a niche, you can position the big guy that way.\</p\>

\<p\>At IBM, we used to pride ourselves on, you know, we have a drop down menu with nine million things on it. And we would talk about how feature-rich our stuff was. But then we would have little guys come in and say, you know what, that is, it\'s complex. There\'s a lot of stuff there you don\'t actually need for certain kind of customers. And trusted by many is this sort of the same thing, saying it\'s slow to innovate. You can\'t change too much, you got all this big existing install base.\</p\>

\<h4\>Example: Canvas vs. D2L\</h4\>

\<p\>Here\'s an example of two companies and how they\'re positioning against each other. So D2L, formerly Desire2Learn, they\'re in the EdTech space. Pretty big company, lots of features, broad, broad product, and does everything that an educational institution would need. A big death breath, it\'s amazing. There\'s little startup pops on the scene called Canvas, and they got a lot of investment money, but the product itself is fairly immature, so it doesn\'t do a lot. And their, their whole thing was ease of use, man. Like we\'re just really sleek, little Volkswagen Beetle. And they\'d come in and they had these slides where they\'re like, here\'s us, we\'re like just the stuff you need and it\'s cool and it\'s modern and it\'s sleek. And those D2L guys, man, they\'re, they\'re like your grandpa\'s station wagon with a bunch of crap hanging off of it. And they literally put on this slide, it was like, yeah, they got all kinds of stuff, but you really want all that stuff? And it was a bit of a punch in the gut, I think, to the bigger company, but smart positioning on the part of the little guy to do that.\</p\>

\<h3\>Strategy 2: Let Them Win Elsewhere\</h3\>

\<p\>Here\'s the other way to beat the giant, and again, we\'re scared to do this sometimes, but the other way you beat the giant is you actually let them win in some places, but you don\'t let them win in your patch. So you got a spot that\'s very well defined where you\'re like, anybody that sits in this patch, we should win those deals. And if it\'s not in this patch, we\'re going to let the big guy have it.\</p\>

\<h4\>Example: CRM for Investment Banking\</h4\>

\<p\>So an example of this, I worked at this company and we were CRM for investment banking. So we were a niche thing inside a bigger, bigger market. And this was ages ago, and Salesforce was still only playing in the small end of the market. We were playing big, big enterprise deals. The leader in that market at the time was this company Siebel, who was way better than us. Like 2 billion revenue, lots and lots of, lots and lots of customers, a big, big company, 8,000 people or something. And we were like 30 people. But we positioned ourselves as CRM for investment banks. So the neat thing about that is it gave us the position, it gave us the ability to position them. So when we went in and met with investment banks, we\'d say, we\'re CRM for investment banks. Investment banks would be all squinty-eyed at us like, wait, wait, don\'t you guys compete with Siebel? And we\'re like, Siebel? What a company, we love them, they\'re amazing. Oh, we love them, they\'re so great, so big, so many customers. They\'re like, you know what they are? They\'re like the world\'s most amazing general purpose CRM for like call centers in India and I don\'t know, manufacturing plants or something. That they\'re amazing if what you want is general purpose CRM, not you, woeful Wall Street, you need something special. And then we had this bunch of special features and we\'d show them like, here\'s all our special stuff. We\'d cut them right out of the deal right at the beginning. No investment banker wants to look like a call center, right? So we would, we would position them in a way where we basically gave them the rest of the market, but in exchange for they\'re never going to beat us on our patch.\</p\>

\<h2\>Competing Against the Ghost (Status Quo)\</h2\>

\<p\>Last one, the ghost. The ghost is in some ways, it\'s like the worst, right? Because you, you did all this effort, all this effort to get on the short list, all this effort to beat the big guy or whatever and get chosen. You\'re all the way down to the end of this deal and then, and then the customer\'s like, no decision, no decision. So I used to work at enterprise software and like, I\'m telling you, the research on this shows that typically in enterprise software, 20% of deals are lost to no decision. That means you went all the way through the whole thing and then right at the end the customer said, eh, we\'re just going to stick with what we got. I actually think that 20% number is low. Like at the companies I worked at, it was closer to 30%. We lost to no decision.\</p\>

\<p\>The data on this is amazing, right? Like it\'s, it, the, um, this stuff on the left of this chart here is from a study that gets done every year where they, they go and talk to successful enterprise sales reps and they talk to them about what do your customers struggle with in the buying process? And the top three things come out like this: number one is building a case for change. How do we actually get the company convinced that they got to do something different? The second one is comparing their options. That\'s all the stuff we\'ve been talking about in the rest of this presentation, right? They don\'t even know how to put on the short list and oh my God, like what if we make the wrong choice here? So we need a good solid way to think about the market so that we know we\'re making the right choice. And then the last one is combating the status quo. That\'s, that\'s the same thing as number one, right? So all of this is like customers don\'t know how to buy and then even when they get all the way down to the end, they have this big trouble switching from what they\'ve got right now.\</p\>

\<h3\>Strategy: Align Positioning with Key Priorities\</h3\>

\<p\>So how do we fix that? And there\'s a bunch of way to do it, but the best way to do it in my opinion is \<em\>you make sure that your positioning aligns with whatever your key priorities are in your target accounts\</em\>. Now this is particularly important to understand right now when, you know, we\'re in the middle of a market that\'s kind of unsettled and a lot of businesses have been impacted by that and a lot of companies are taking a good hard look at their priorities. A lot of them have shifted their priorities because of COVID.\</p\>

\<h4\>Example: Sampler and Contactless Product Sampling\</h4\>

\<p\>So here\'s an example of a company that in my opinion did a really masterful job of this, and I\'m super biased because I happen to sit on a board of this company, but it\'s a great example. So these folks are Sampler. What they do is they have a digital product sampling solution. So if you think about big consumer packaged goods brands, they spend a fortune, like billions and millions of dollars on sampling campaigns. So you\'re launching a new granola bar or cereal or snack or something, and you\'ll see these people standing on the street corner handing out samples or they\'re in the grocery store handing out samples or they\'re in the drugstore handing out samples. Companies spend billions on that, and it is a key part of their product launch strategy. The way it\'s done today is super inefficient. They have no idea who took the sample, they don\'t know it really, they can\'t trace if sales went up because of it, and what kind of people like the sample. Sampler does this in a digital way. They match people to samples, they deliver the sample to their home, and then the brands can follow up with them and survey them and figure out their demographics and find out did they like it or not, did they go buy things? So that\'s what Sampler does. Everything\'s going good, company\'s growing good, everything\'s good. COVID hits.\</p\>

\<p\>So and the immediate reaction inside their customer base was sampling\'s done, it\'s done, it\'s over. We can\'t stand on the street corner and hand these things out. We sure as heck aren\'t going to be in a hazmat suit in the middle of the grocery store handing out little bits of piece of chocolate bar. No, it\'s done, over.
