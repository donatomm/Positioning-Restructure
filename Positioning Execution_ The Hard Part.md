## **April Dunford's Positioning Framework Is Right. The Hard Part Starts After the Workshop.**

This is a summary/takeaways section provided by Gemini 3 Flash (Gem) by DM

## **TL;DR:**

- Positioning is a strategy problem that quickly evolves into a \"memory problem\" as context fails to transfer from workshops to executors.

- The \"context gap\" occurs because writers and sales teams lack the background on rejected alternatives and executive reasoning found in the strategy room.

- AI's greatest potential in marketing isn\'t just speed, but its ability to encode and enforce positioning strategy across all content production and review processes.

## **Detailed Summary:**

### **The Post-Workshop Collapse**

Daniel Hebert argues that while April Dunford's positioning framework is effective, the strategy often \"dies\" during execution. The core issue is the transition from a high-level workshop to the daily reality of updating hundreds of assets, blog posts, and sales materials. This creates a \"context gap\" where the nuance of the strategy is lost on the people actually creating the content.

### **The Role of AI as Strategic Guardrail**

The discussion highlights a shift in how AI should be utilized. Rather than just a tool for faster writing, Hebert and contributors suggest using AI to \"reduce the distance\" between strategy and execution. By feeding positioning docs into AI tools, companies can ensure that every piece of content---regardless of who writes it---stays aligned with the original market thesis and comparison sets.

## **Key Takeaways:**

### **Strategic Execution**

- **The Context Gap:** Strategy lives in the heads of the creators; execution is handed to those who weren\'t in the room. Documentation alone is insufficient to transfer the \"why\" behind strategic choices.

- **Ranking by Revenue:** When updating content to reflect new positioning, teams should prioritize by revenue impact rather than publication date.

### **Organizational Alignment**

- **Internalization Over Deliverables:** Positioning is only successful if the sales team actually uses it in live conversations. If it remains a \"whiteboard exercise,\" it fails to impact sales velocity.

- **The \"Living\" Document:** Positioning must be treated as a continuous journey. As markets shift and competitors evolve, the positioning must be updated and re-verified through customer interactions.

[**[Daniel Hebert]{.underline}**](https://www.google.com/search?q=https://www.linkedin.com/in/daniel-hebert/)

Content ops for scaling SaaS teams \| Founder, Oleno & SalesMVP Lab \| 13 years building revenue engines

April Dunford\'s framework is right, and most teams still stop too early.

I\'ve done April\'s positioning work twice, at LevelJump and Proposify. The workshop part was excellent. You leave with sharper answers to the questions buyers are already asking: what is this, compared to what, and why should I care?

That alone fixes a lot. If your category, comparison set, unique attributes, value, and target customer actually fit together, sales gets clearer fast. Your homepage gets simpler. Content has a better chance of connecting to pipeline instead of just collecting traffic.

But the positioning workshop is not the finish line. It\'s the handoff.

And honestly, that\'s where a lot of good positioning dies.

A few execs and product marketing leaders spend a week or two getting to a strong point of view, then that strategy has to make its way into 200 old blog posts, 14 sales assets, nurture emails, comparison pages, paid landing pages, social, and whatever your content team is supposed to ship next Tuesday.

That\'s a different problem entirely.

Writers have the hardest job in the whole transition. They weren\'t in the room for the debate. They didn\'t hear the executive arguments, the customer evidence, the rejected alternatives, or the reasoning behind why one comparison set was right and another one would confuse the buyer. They get a messaging doc and are expected to produce sharp judgment from it.

Usually, they can\'t. Not because they\'re weak. Because context doesn\'t transfer cleanly through a doc.

Positioning work creates a context gap. The strategy lives in the heads of the people who made it, while execution gets handed to the people who didn\'t.

Once you have dozens of assets, multiple contributors, and weekly publishing pressure, documentation alone starts to leak. The nuance goes first.

So what should happen after the workshop?

A few things, in order:

- test the positioning in sales conversations first

- audit existing content against the new frame

- rank what to rewrite by revenue impact, not by publish date

- encode the comparison set, target customer, category, and value claims into the content process itself

- make review about strategic fit, not just grammar and brand voice

That\'s where I think AI gets interesting.

Not because it writes faster. That\'s useful, but not the main thing.

The real value is reducing the distance between strategy and execution. If your positioning can be encoded into the system that reviews, rewrites, and produces content, your team doesn\'t have to rely on every writer carrying the whole market thesis in their head. The judgment gets more consistent. Old content gets easier to triage. New content is less likely to drift back to the vague story you just worked so hard to escape.

Positioning is a strategy problem first. Then it becomes a memory problem.

Most teams solve the first one and underestimate the second.
